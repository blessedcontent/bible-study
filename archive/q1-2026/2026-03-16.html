Monday, Parents and Children

Colossians · Week 12, Day 3
March 16, 2026
✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR

Paul gives children one command (obey) and fathers one warning (do not crush their spirit). The asymmetry is the point.

  • ☩ Children are told to obey their parents "in everything," a sweeping command qualified by the same "in the Lord" framework that governs the entire household code.
  • ☩ Fathers receive a single, surgically specific prohibition: do not exasperate your children, so that they won't become discouraged.
  • ☩ The word Paul uses for "discouraged" means to lose heart, to become spiritless. His concern is not rebellion but resignation: the child who simply gives up.
  • ☩ Supporting passages from Proverbs and Deuteronomy fill out the picture: parenting is not just correction but intentional, all-of-life spiritual formation.
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday Paul addressed the marriage relationship: wives aligning voluntarily under the Lord's authority, husbands loving sacrificially and guarding against bitterness. Today he moves to the second ring of the household: parents and children. The pattern is the same. Paul addresses both sides of the power dynamic. He gives the weaker party a command (obey), and the stronger party a warning (don't crush). If yesterday was about how Christ reshapes the most intimate partnership, today is about how Christ reshapes the most formative one.

🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
A Roman Father's Absolute Power Over His Children

Under Roman law, the patria potestas ("fatherly power") gave a father legal authority over his children that lasted their entire lives, not just until adulthood. A Roman father could decide whether a newborn would be raised or exposed (abandoned). He could sell his children, arrange their marriages, and control their property even after they had families of their own. Discipline was his prerogative, and there was no legal recourse for children who were treated harshly. This was not abuse by Roman standards; it was simply the way households worked.

Into this world Paul writes something that would have sounded strange to a Roman father: do not exasperate your children. The idea that a father's authority could be exercised in a way that was wrong, not legally but morally, was not part of the cultural furniture. Paul is not abolishing parental authority. He is putting a conscience inside it. The goal of Christian fatherhood is not obedient children at any cost; it is children whose spirits are intact, whose hearts have not been crushed by the very people God assigned to protect them.

📖 Into the Word

Today's anchor passage is Colossians 3:20-21, Paul's paired instructions to children and fathers. These two verses establish a principle that cuts against both ancient and modern assumptions: children owe obedience, but parents owe their children an environment where obedience does not cost them their soul. The supporting passages from Proverbs and Deuteronomy show that Scripture has always envisioned parenting as an all-of-life, spiritually intentional practice, not just rule enforcement.

Colossians 3:20
☩ Open in Bible App
"Children, obey your parents in everything, for this pleases the Lord."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The command is comprehensive: "in everything" (kata panta, κατὰ πάντα). Paul does not leave room for selective obedience based on a child's preferences. But notice the motivation: "for this pleases the Lord." The Greek euareston (εὐάρεστον) means "well-pleasing" or "acceptable." Paul grounds obedience not in the parent's authority or the child's fear, but in something higher: it matters to God. A child obeying parents is not merely maintaining household order; it is an act of worship. The implicit "in the Lord" framework from verse 18 still governs: this obedience operates within the boundaries of what is consistent with following Christ. Paul is forming children who obey because they understand they are serving someone greater than their parents.
Colossians 3:21
☩ Open in Bible App
"Fathers, do not exasperate your children, so that they won't become discouraged."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
This is the most important verse in the passage, and it is aimed squarely at the person with the most power. The Greek erethizo (ἐρεθίζω) means to provoke, to irritate, to stir up. Paul is describing a pattern, not a single incident: the father who constantly criticizes, who sets impossible standards, who corrects without encouragement, who uses authority as a weapon rather than a tool. The result Paul fears is devastating: athumeo (ἀθυμέω), to become dispirited, to lose heart. This is not a child who rebels. It is a child who gives up. The spirit goes out of them. They stop trying because they have learned that nothing they do will ever be enough. Paul knew that the most dangerous thing a father can do is not fail to discipline his children; it is to discipline them in a way that kills their desire to grow. The Ephesians parallel (Ephesians 6:4) adds the positive counterpart: "bring them up in the training and instruction of the Lord."
Proverbs 22:6
☩ Open in Bible App
"Start a youth out on his way; even when he grows old he will not depart from it."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The Hebrew word translated "start" is chanak (חָנַךְ), which literally means "to dedicate" or "to initiate," the same word used for dedicating a building. The idea is not merely teaching information but consecrating a young person to a way of life. The CSB captures this well: "start a youth out on his way." This is a proverb, not an iron-clad promise; it describes the general principle that early formation shapes the trajectory of a life. Parents are not just managing behavior; they are launching a human being in a direction.
Proverbs 22:15
☩ Open in Bible App
"Foolishness is bound to the heart of a youth; a rod of discipline will separate it from him."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
This proverb acknowledges a reality every parent knows: children are not born wise. The word "bound" (qashar) means tied or attached; foolishness clings to the young heart like a vine. Discipline is the tool that separates the foolishness from the child. But read in light of Colossians 3:21, the method matters. The "rod of discipline" in Proverbs is not a license for harshness; it is a metaphor for correction applied with wisdom. Paul's warning against exasperation is the guardrail that keeps correction from becoming destruction.
Deuteronomy 6:6-7
☩ Open in Bible App
"These words that I am giving you today are to be in your heart. Repeat them to your children. Talk about them when you sit in your house and when you walk along the road, when you lie down and when you get up."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Moses paints a picture of spiritual formation that is not a scheduled event but a way of life. The words of God are to be woven into every ordinary moment: at home, on the road, at bedtime, in the morning. The Hebrew shanan (שָׁנַן) translated "repeat" means to sharpen by repetition, like whetting a blade. This is not a lecture delivered once a week; it is a constant, natural, integrated conversation about God that happens because faith saturates the parent's own life first. Notice the order: the words must be "in your heart" before they reach your children's ears. You cannot transmit what you do not possess.
Proverbs 1:8-9
☩ Open in Bible App
"Listen, my son, to your father's instruction, and don't reject your mother's teaching, for they will be a garland of favor on your head and pendants around your neck."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Solomon addresses both parents' roles: the father instructs, the mother teaches. The metaphor of a "garland" and "pendants" is striking. In the ancient world, garlands and jewelry signaled honor and status. Solomon is saying that a child who receives and keeps their parents' wisdom will wear it like a crown. Parental instruction is not a burden to escape; it is a gift that adorns the person who carries it into adulthood.
Matthew 19:14
☩ Open in Bible App
"Jesus said, 'Leave the children alone, and don't try to keep them from coming to me, because the kingdom of heaven belongs to such as these.'"
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Jesus rebukes the disciples for treating children as interruptions. In the kingdom of God, children are not obstacles to ministry; they are models of the kind of trust the kingdom requires. Jesus does not merely tolerate children; he claims them. Any parenting approach that pushes children away from Jesus, whether through harshness, hypocrisy, or spiritual pressure that makes faith feel like a prison, contradicts the heart of the One who said the kingdom belongs to them.
🎯 Street Level
The Thermostat and the Thermometer

Every home has a thermostat and a thermometer. The thermometer reads the temperature; it tells you what the environment already is. The thermostat sets the temperature; it determines what the environment will become. Children are thermometers. They reflect the emotional and spiritual climate of the home. If a child is withdrawn, anxious, or angry, they are often reading the room accurately. Parents are thermostats. They set the temperature. When Paul tells fathers not to exasperate their children, he is saying: check your thermostat. If your children are losing heart, the problem may not be their attitude. It may be the climate you are creating. A home where correction comes without encouragement, where standards exist without warmth, where a child never hears "I'm proud of you" but constantly hears "that's not good enough," is a home where the thermostat is set to discourage.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
The Longitudinal Data on Harsh Parenting

A longitudinal study published in early 2025, tracking more than 4,200 Brazilian children from birth to age 18, found that children raised by harsh parents showed measurably worse outcomes across multiple dimensions by the time they reached adulthood. They had lower self-esteem, poorer ability to regulate their emotions, fewer prosocial behaviors, and more relationship problems with their peers. The researchers noted that even moderate harshness, not just severe abuse, produced these effects.

A separate study from the University of Cambridge and University College Dublin, using data from over 7,500 Irish children, reached a similar conclusion: children exposed to hostile parenting at age three were 1.5 times more likely to be in the high-risk category for mental health problems by age nine. Hostile parenting in the study included frequent shouting, routine physical punishment, isolating children when they misbehave, and punishing based on the parent's mood rather than the child's behavior.

Paul wrote one sentence: "Fathers, do not exasperate your children, so that they won't become discouraged." Modern developmental psychology has spent decades producing the evidence for why that sentence matters. The Greek word athumeo, to become spiritless, is not a theological abstraction. It is a clinical reality that researchers are now documenting in data sets of thousands.

✝️ The Christ Connection

Jesus never exasperated the people he was forming. He corrected Peter without crushing him. He challenged the rich young ruler without shaming him. He wept over Jerusalem without giving up on it. When his disciples failed spectacularly, falling asleep in the garden, denying him by the fire, scattering at the cross, he came back after the resurrection and restored them. That is the model for Christian parenting: correction that preserves dignity, discipline that builds rather than breaks, and a relentless refusal to give up on the person God entrusted to your care. Fathers are not called to be God in their children's lives; they are called to point to the God who is already there.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
If you have children (of any age), ask yourself Paul's question honestly: is anything I am doing causing discouragement in my child's spirit? If you are not sure, ask them. And if you are not a parent, think about a young person you influence: a niece, a nephew, a student, a mentee. What is the temperature you are setting in that relationship?
🙏 Closing Prayer

Lord, you are the Father from whom every family on earth derives its name. You know what it means to love a child who does not always listen, who wanders, who makes choices that break your heart. And you never once crushed our spirit in the process of correcting our path. Teach us to parent the way you parent: with truth and tenderness, with standards and grace, with discipline that builds and never destroys. Forgive us for the times we have set the thermostat to frustration instead of faith. Show us the children in our lives who are losing heart, and give us the courage to change the climate before it is too late. In Jesus' name, Amen.

💬 Discussion Question
Paul's warning is not about a child who rebels but about a child who gives up. What is the difference between a discouraged child and a disobedient child, and which one should concern a parent more?
