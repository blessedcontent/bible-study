Colossians · Week 12, Day 2

📌 TL;DR

Paul's instructions to husbands and wives are not a power manual; they are a portrait of what marriage looks like when Christ is actually Lord of the household.

  • ☩ Paul addresses wives and husbands as moral agents with distinct responsibilities, not as ruler and subject in a hierarchy of worth.
  • ☩ The wife's submission is qualified by a phrase no Roman philosopher would have added: "as is fitting in the Lord." Her loyalty to Christ comes first.
  • ☩ The husband's command is not to lead, manage, or control, but to love, and to not become bitter. Bitterness is the silent killer Paul specifically names.
  • ☩ Ephesians 5 expands the picture: a husband's love is to mirror Christ's sacrificial love for the church, making himself the one who gives, not the one who takes.
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday's Sabbath opener framed the big picture: Paul is now taking the theology of the new life (the virtues, the peace, the word dwelling richly) and walking it into the closest human relationships. He is testing whether the new self can survive the kitchen table. Today he starts with the most intimate relationship of all: marriage. The compassion, kindness, humility, and love Paul told us to "put on" in Colossians 3:12-14 now meet their first concrete test.

🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
What "Submit" Meant Before Paul Rewrote It

Roman marriage in the first century was a legal and economic arrangement. A wife was transferred from her father's authority to her husband's. The Greco-Roman "household codes" (Haustafeln) that philosophers like Aristotle had established addressed three relationships: husband-wife, father-child, master-slave. But they only addressed the person in power. The weaker party was simply expected to comply. No philosopher wrote to the husband telling him what he owed his wife. No one told the master what he owed his slave.

Paul's household code is structurally similar, but the content is an earthquake. He addresses both parties in every relationship. He places limits on authority. He introduces the phrase "in the Lord" as a governing qualifier that redefines every instruction. When Paul tells wives to submit "as is fitting in the Lord," he is not rubber-stamping Roman patriarchy. He is subverting it from the inside by making Christ, not the husband, the ultimate authority in the marriage.

📖 Into the Word

Today's anchor passage is Colossians 3:18-19, Paul's compact instructions to wives and husbands. These two verses are deceptively brief, but they carry enormous weight because they redefine the most powerful relationship in the ancient household. The supporting passage from Ephesians 5 expands the picture, revealing the theological foundation Paul is building on: Christ's love for the church as the model for marital love.

Colossians 3:18
☩ Open in Bible App
"Wives, submit yourselves to your husbands, as is fitting in the Lord."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The Greek word here is hypotasso (ὑποτάσσω), a military term meaning "to arrange under." But in the middle voice Paul uses, it carries the sense of voluntary alignment, not forced subordination. A wife voluntarily arranges herself within the marriage relationship. The critical qualifier is the last phrase: "as is fitting in the Lord." The Greek aneko (ἀνῆκεν) means "what is proper" or "what is appropriate." Paul is not issuing an unlimited command. He is saying this arrangement is fitting specifically within the framework of life "in the Lord." If a husband asks something that contradicts the Lord's will, the wife's first allegiance is clear. This is revolutionary: it places a moral ceiling on the husband's authority that no Roman household code recognized.
Colossians 3:19
☩ Open in Bible App
"Husbands, love your wives and don't be bitter toward them."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul's command to husbands is stunning in its simplicity and its sting. The verb is agapao (ἀγαπάω), the same self-giving, sacrificial love that describes God's love for the world in John 3:16. This is not romantic affection or sexual desire; it is a love that chooses the other's good at personal cost. Then Paul adds a prohibition that feels almost too specific: "don't be bitter" (me pikrainesthe, μὴ πικραίνεσθε). The word pikraino means to make sharp, to embitter, to become harsh. Paul could have said "don't be angry" or "don't be controlling." Instead, he targets bitterness, the slow-building resentment that curdles a marriage from the inside. It is the acid that forms when a husband feels disrespected, inconvenienced, or unappreciated, and instead of addressing it, lets it ferment. Paul names it because he knows it is the real killer.
Ephesians 5:22-25
☩ Open in Bible App
"Wives, submit to your own husbands as to the Lord, because the husband is the head of the wife as Christ is the head of the church. He is the Savior of the body. Now as the church submits to Christ, so also wives are to submit to their husbands in everything. Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ loved the church and gave himself for her."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Ephesians unpacks what Colossians compresses. The standard for a husband's love is not his feelings, his preferences, or his culture's expectations. It is the Cross. Christ loved the church by giving himself up for her. That is the measuring stick. Notice that when Paul defines "headship," his illustration is not a boss in an office or a general commanding troops. It is a Savior who died. If the husband's love does not include sacrifice, self-denial, and the willingness to put his wife's flourishing above his own comfort, it has not yet reached the standard Paul is setting. This verse makes the husband the servant, not the CEO.
Ephesians 5:33
☩ Open in Bible App
"To sum up, each one of you is to love his wife as himself, and the wife is to respect her husband."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul closes the Ephesians passage with a summary that reveals the mutual nature of what he is describing. The husband's assignment is love; the wife's is respect. Neither instruction is a blank check. Both are bounded by the Christ-centered framework Paul has been building. The word for "respect" here is phobeo (φοβέω), often translated "reverence," which carries the idea of taking someone seriously, honoring their role and their person. Paul envisions a marriage where both partners are actively giving what the other most needs.
🎯 Street Level
The Co-Pilot Seat

Think about the difference between a passenger and a co-pilot. A passenger sits in the back, disengaged, along for the ride, with no say in where the plane goes. A co-pilot sits right next to the pilot, monitors the same instruments, communicates constantly, and can take the controls when needed. They are not in competition. They are not identical in function. But they are equals in the cockpit, and the plane does not fly without both of them fully engaged. Paul is not describing a passenger-pilot marriage. He is describing a cockpit where two people are deeply attentive to each other and jointly committed to getting the plane where God wants it to go. The husband's "headship" is not about rank; it is about responsibility. The wife's "submission" is not about silence; it is about trust. Both are accountable to the same Lord who is watching the flight.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
What Four Decades of Marriage Research Confirms

Psychologist John Gottman has spent over 40 years studying what makes marriages last. His research at the University of Washington's "Love Lab," involving more than 3,000 couples in longitudinal studies, identified with remarkable accuracy the patterns that predict whether a marriage will thrive or fail. Among his most well-known findings: contempt is the single greatest predictor of divorce. Not conflict itself, but contempt, the expression of superiority and disdain toward a partner. Gottman's research team found they could predict divorce with 94% accuracy simply by observing how couples handled a 15-minute conversation about a disagreement.

What Paul calls "bitterness" (pikraino) in Colossians 3:19 is the seed from which contempt grows. Gottman's research confirms what Paul knew two thousand years ago: the real threats to a marriage are not the big explosions but the slow corrosion of resentment, the eye rolls, the sarcasm, the quiet withdrawal. Gottman found that successful couples maintain a ratio of at least five positive interactions for every one negative interaction. Couples heading for divorce show ratios closer to 0.8 to 1. Paul's prescription is simpler but aims at the same target: love actively, and root out bitterness before it takes hold.

Paul did not have a research lab. He had the Holy Spirit and a chain. But the instruction he wrote from a Roman prison aligns precisely with what four decades of empirical observation has confirmed: marriages survive on active love and die on accumulated bitterness.

✝️ The Christ Connection

The reason Paul's marriage instructions work is that they are not, ultimately, about marriage. They are about Christ. When Paul tells husbands to love like Christ loved the church, he is saying that every Christian marriage is meant to be a living portrait of the gospel. The husband's sacrificial love mirrors Jesus giving himself for the church. The wife's willing trust mirrors the church's response to a Savior who has proven himself worthy. When a marriage reflects this pattern, the neighbors do not just see a happy couple. They see a miniature display of the gospel in action: two people rehearsing the love story that saved the world.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
If you are married, do a quiet audit tonight: is there any bitterness toward your spouse that you have been carrying but not addressing? Name it honestly before God. If you are single, ask yourself the same question about your closest relationship. Bitterness is not a marriage-only problem; it is a human problem.
🙏 Closing Prayer

Lord, you designed marriage to tell a story bigger than two people. Forgive us for the ways we have made it about power, about winning arguments, about keeping score. Show us the bitterness we have been storing, the contempt we have disguised as humor, the love we have withheld because we felt we were owed something first. Teach husbands to love the way Jesus loved: with sacrifice and without conditions. Teach wives to trust the way the church is learning to trust: with courage and without losing their voice. And for all of us, married or not, let every relationship we have reflect the self-giving love that took you to the Cross. In Jesus' name, Amen.
