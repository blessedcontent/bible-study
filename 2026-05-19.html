Faith Is Not a Feeling

Growing in a Relationship with God Lesson 8, Having Faith Part 4 of 7
May 19, 2026
✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR

A mustard seed sits in the palm of a Galilean fisherman, barely visible against the calluses of his hand, and Jesus tells him that faith this small in a God this real moves mountains we have been giving up on for years.

  • ☩Faith is not a feeling that runs hot and cold with the weather of the soul. In its first-century Greek, the word for it meant the steady loyalty you would lend money on, the contract you would sign in good faith, the soldier whose fidelity did not break.
  • ☩Jesus diagnosed the failed exorcism at the foot of the Mount of Transfiguration with one word He reserved for His own followers: "little-faithedness." Not too little quantity. The wrong quality. A mustard seed of real faith moves the mountain that a boulder of pretended faith never touches.
  • ☩Faith is not even ours to manufacture. Ephesians 2:8 calls it the gift of God; Romans 12:3 says He has dealt to each one a measure of it. We are not asked to generate faith. We are asked to spend what He has already deposited.
  • ☩Today's challenge is to take four short verses, put them on our lips, and say them out loud as a prayer. Faith is exercised by speaking, not by feeling.
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday we watched Jesus read faith in three people no one else in the room would have praised: a centurion at a distance, a Canaanite mother on the road, a father with tears in his eyes. The text never tells us how any of them felt. It tells us what they did. The centurion sent for Him. The mother chased Him down. The father said the six most honest words in the gospels. Today's question presses on that silence. Did they feel the kind of warm certainty we often associate with the word "faith"? Probably not. They felt the cold weather of impossible diagnoses, terrified mothers, and humiliated dads. What they had was not a feeling; it was a posture toward Someone. And that is what the Bible calls faith.

🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
The Word in Their Contracts

Walk through any Greek marketplace in the first century and you would hear the word pistis a dozen times before lunch. A merchant insisting his goods had been sold in good faith. A wife described as piste, faithful, by her husband at the well. A soldier praised for his fidelity to his commander. A judge admitting that he could not find a witness's testimony trustworthy. In the courts of Athens, Corinth, and Ephesus, pistis was a daily working word, and what it meant everywhere was the steady loyalty that does not break when the weather turns. The kind of person you would lend money to without a witness because their word was as good as their bond. Paul, writing in the same language to the same culture, did not invent the term. He used it in its everyday market sense and applied it to God. Faith in the Bible's sense is the same kind of loyalty, redirected.

In the same Greek world, the words for the emotional and sensory side of life were different. Pathos, passion. Aisthesis, sensation. The Stoics taught apatheia, freedom from being ruled by the weather of the feelings. The Bible's contribution was not to side with the Stoics against the heart, but to clarify what faith actually is. The God of the Bible welcomes feelings. The Psalms are full of them. But He does not require them as the condition for our relationship with Him. Faith is closer to the marketplace's pistis than to the heart's pathos. It is a stable, directional commitment to a Person, exercised on Tuesdays when we feel nothing and Sundays when the music is making us cry. The Father is not measuring the temperature in our chest. He is reading the loyalty in our orientation. That is good news for anyone who has ever wondered whether their faith was the right shape, because the shape He is looking for is one we already know from every faithful human relationship we have ever been part of.

📖 Into the Word

It is the day after the Mount of Transfiguration. Jesus has come down with Peter, James, and John from the highest moment of His earthly ministry and walked straight into the lowest moment of His disciples' ministry. A father is standing in the road with a tormented son, and the nine disciples who stayed at the base of the mountain have just publicly failed to help him. Jesus drives the demon out in a sentence, hands the boy back to his father, and turns to the nine. Privately, they ask Him why they could not do it. The answer is short and exact and contains the verse the whole rest of today is built on. The problem was not the size of their faith. The problem was the kind.

Matthew 17:20
☩ Open in Bible App
"'Because of your little faith,' he told them. 'For truly I tell you, if you have faith the size of a mustard seed, you will tell this mountain, "Move from here to there," and it will move. Nothing will be impossible for you.'"
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

The Greek word Matthew puts on Jesus' lips for "little faith" is oligopistia. It is a compound of oligos (few, small) and pistis, and Jesus uses it almost exclusively of His own followers. Not the Pharisees, who refused signs altogether. Not the crowds, who came for bread. His own disciples, the men who had walked with Him for two years and just watched Him feed five thousand people from five loaves. The diagnosis was not aimed at outsiders. It was aimed inside the camp. The most disclosing word in the verse is that the men whose faith was rebuked had plenty of religious volume; what they were short on was the right substance.

Then the image. A mustard seed. Kokkos sinapeos. The smallest of garden seeds in first-century Palestine, the visual shorthand a rabbi would use when he meant "barely there." Jesus had used the same seed in Matthew 13:31-32 to picture the kingdom of God, which begins as something a farmer would not bother to weigh and ends up as a tree large enough for the birds of the air to roost in. Today He returns to the same seed for a different point. A mustard seed of real faith does what a boulder of pretended faith never touches.

Notice what Jesus does not say. He does not say, "If you had more faith, you would move mountains." He says, "If you had faith the size of a mustard seed." The disciples almost certainly heard the comment as a quantity comment. We almost certainly do too. But the noun He used for their problem was qualitative. Oligopistia is not "small faith"; it is "wrong-kind-of-faith." A seed of the real thing is alive. A pile of the wrong thing, no matter how impressive, is dead matter on the ground.

The mountain in the verse is metaphor with teeth. In rabbinic literature, the phrase "to remove mountains" meant to do what was widely judged to be impossible. The standing rabbinic compliment for an exceptional teacher was that he could "uproot mountains" by his interpretive skill. Jesus takes the metaphor and points it back at His disciples. The mountain you cannot move is the demon you cannot drive out. The mountain is the daughter who will not come home. The mountain is the marriage that has been petrified for fifteen years. The mountain is the addiction your willpower has not budged. Small faith in a great God moves what large faith in nothing has never touched.

Ephesians 2:8
☩ Open in Bible App
"For you are saved by grace through faith, and this is not from yourselves; it is God's gift."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

Paul writes this to a Gentile church in a Roman port city whose members had spent most of their lives worshiping mystery cults and emperor statues. He chooses every word with care. We are saved by grace, the source. Through faith, the means. And this is not from yourselves, the disclaimer. It is God's gift, the explanation. The grammar is doing real work. Grace is the cause; faith is the channel; neither is a contribution we put on the table.

The verse closes a long debate before it even starts. If faith is the means by which grace reaches us, where did the faith come from? Romans 12:3 answers in the same direction: God has dealt to each one a measure of faith. The picture in Paul's mind is a parent placing the first coin in a child's open hand. The faith we exercise is the same faith we have received. The Holy Spirit, in the moment we first heard the gospel and the message landed, deposited in us the response that received it. Jeremiah 31:3 describes that movement from God's side: "I have loved you with an everlasting love; therefore I have continued to extend faithful love to you." The drawing is His; the responding is the answer His drawing makes possible.

This dissolves the most common modern objection to faith. The person who says, "I just do not have enough faith," is, in the Bible's grammar, complaining about a gift that has been given. The honest version of the objection, as Mark 9:24 showed us yesterday, is not "I do not have any" but "I do believe; help my unbelief." The first sentence locks the door. The second prays it open.

Luke 7:50
☩ Open in Bible App
"And he said to the woman, 'Your faith has saved you. Go in peace.'"
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

The setting is a Pharisee's house in the middle of dinner, a woman known in the town as a sinner has just washed Jesus' feet with her tears, dried them with her hair, kissed them, and anointed them with perfume. The host is appalled. The other guests are murmuring. Jesus is calm. And after a parable about forgiven debts He turns to her and says one of the most disclosing lines in Luke's gospel: he pistis sou sesoken se. "Your faith has saved you." The verb sesoken is a perfect tense, meaning the saving has happened and the result is standing in the room. She came in already saved by what her faith had received from Him.

Notice what was visible in her faith that night. Not eloquence. She does not say a word. Not theological precision. The Pharisees who knew theology were the ones who could not see what was happening. Not even certainty about the future. Her past life was the loudest thing in the room. What she had was a posture of love and trust toward Jesus that her body could not stop showing. She wept on His feet. Her faith was a person on her knees. The same passages this week (Hebrews 12:1-2, the cloud of witnesses inviting us to run while we keep our eyes on Jesus, the founder and perfecter of our faith; 2 Chronicles 15:7, the Spirit's word to a king to be strong because his work would not be in vain; Romans 3:23-26, Paul's compressed gospel that God is both just and the justifier of the one who has faith in Jesus) are not propositions that need to be felt to be true. They are deeds we hold in our hands the way the woman held the alabaster jar. Faith is what we do with what we know.

🎯 Street Level
The Marriage at the Sink

It is their twenty-third anniversary. They are not staring at each other across a candlelit table. They are doing dishes side by side in the kitchen at nine on a Thursday night, after a hard week. He is tired; she is tired. There were no flowers today because both of them forgot, and neither one is going to bring it up because neither one minds. He hands her a plate; she dries it; she slots it into the cabinet without thinking. He says, "I'll start the coffee tomorrow." She says, "Okay, I'll make the lunches." That is the marriage. The thirty seconds at the sink. The feeling that took them up the aisle two decades ago has not visited them in three months and will probably visit again in a few weeks. Neither one of them is leaving. Neither one is even worried about leaving. The bond is not in their chests. The bond is in their feet. They have walked toward each other across a thousand kitchens like this one, until the walking became the marriage.

Faith in the Bible's sense is what is happening at the sink. It is the steady, contracted loyalty of a person to a Person, not the temperature in our chest at a given moment. The centurion did not feel close to Jesus the morning his servant fell sick. He sent for Him anyway. The Canaanite mother did not feel welcomed on the road to Tyre. She kept walking anyway. The father at the bottom of the mountain did not feel sure. He said the six honest words out loud anyway. A mustard seed of faith in a great God is a couple at the sink doing one more night of dishes together. A boulder of feeling without that loyalty is a candlelit dinner where one of them is already on the way out the door.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
Act First, the Feeling Follows

In June 2025, the Journal of Medical Internet Research published a systematic review of seventeen randomized controlled trials on a specific kind of depression therapy: behavioral activation. The treatment is built on a counterintuitive finding clinicians have been documenting since the 1970s and which now sits inside every modern CBT manual. People who wait until they feel like doing something to do it do not get better. The depression keeps the feeling buried under itself. What works, in trial after trial, is the reverse. The patient and the therapist write down five small things the patient used to do when life was normal: a walk to the mailbox, a phone call to one friend, fifteen minutes of dishes, a shower before noon. The patient is asked to do them on the calendar regardless of whether they feel like it. The feeling shows up in week six or seven, not before. The 2025 meta-analysis found significant reduction in depression at two, three, and six months. The "feel first, then act" sequence does not work. The "act first, the feeling follows" sequence does.

This is the architecture of biblical faith. The centurion did not feel close to Jesus the morning his servant fell sick. He sent the messengers anyway. The Canaanite mother did not feel welcomed on the road to Tyre. She kept walking anyway. The father at the bottom of the mountain did not feel sure. He said the six honest words out loud anyway. Twenty-first-century clinical research is catching up to what the Bible has been telling tired Christians for two thousand years. Bring the mustard seed you actually have. Act on it. The feeling will not be the part of you that meets Him at the end of the obedience. He will.

✝️ The Christ Connection

The mustard seed becomes the cross. On Friday afternoon the disciples watched the smallest visible defeat in human history. Their Rabbi, who had multiplied bread and stopped storms and called dead children out of their graves, was nailed to a beam outside the city and stopped moving by mid-afternoon. From the road below it looked like the end of a movement. It was the seed in the ground. The Father had asked the Son for a faith that would not see, would not feel, would not have any verifiable confirmation in the moment, would only have a promise spoken into darkness and the loyalty of a Son to a Father who had not yet acted. And the Son trusted. By Sunday morning, that mustard-seed faith of the Son had moved the mountain of death itself. Hebrews calls Him the "founder and perfecter of our faith." He went first into the unfelt night and came back out so that we would never have to walk by feeling alone. The faith we exercise on dry Tuesdays is the same kind He exercised on the cross, on a smaller scale, with the same outcome promised.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Take four short verses and say them out loud today as a prayer. Hebrews 12:1-2: "Let us also lay aside every hindrance and the sin that so easily ensnares us. Let us run with endurance the race that lies before us, keeping our eyes on Jesus, the source and perfecter of our faith. For the joy that lay before him, he endured the cross, despising the shame, and sat down at the right hand of the throne of God." 2 Chronicles 15:7: "But as for you, be strong; don't give up, for your work has a reward." Romans 3:23-26 in summary: I have sinned and fall short, but I am declared righteous as a gift through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus, whom God set forth as the satisfaction of His justice by His blood. Luke 7:50: "Your faith has saved you. Go in peace." Do not wait until you feel the verses. Speak them, and let the speaking exercise the faith.
🙏 Closing Prayer

Father, we have lived too long under the lie that says our faith is only as real as our feelings about it. Forgive us where we have measured Your nearness by the weather of our hearts. Forgive us where we have stopped praying because we did not feel like praying, and stopped showing up because we did not feel like showing up. Teach us this morning that the mustard seed in our hand is enough, because of the soil it has been planted in. Help us to remember that the centurion did not need to feel close to be heard, and the Canaanite mother did not need to feel welcomed to be answered, and the father at the bottom of the mountain did not need to feel certain to receive the healing of his son. We bring You the small, real, sometimes shivering loyalty we actually have, and we ask You to do with it what only You can. Move the mountains we have given up on. In Jesus' name, Amen.

💬 Discussion Question
"Where in your life have you been waiting to feel faith before you acted in faith (a prayer you have stopped praying because it stopped feeling answered, a forgiveness you have not extended because you did not feel ready, a worship moment you skipped because you did not feel inspired)? What is one specific, small, unfelt act of trust you could take this week as your mustard seed, knowing that the mountain does not move by your temperature but by the One you are trusting?"
