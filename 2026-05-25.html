Strongholds in My Relationship With God

Growing in a Relationship with God Lesson 9, Sin, the Gospel, and the Law Part 3 of 7
May 25, 2026
✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR

Jesus picks up the same eye that took Samson down and prescribes a surgery so radical the only word for it is "extreme."

  • ☩Strongholds are the entrenched compromises we have made peace with: the lust, the resentment, the trumpet-blast of our own goodness, the quiet contempt for the person at work we love to judge.
  • ☩Jesus did not soften His prescription. Hand, foot, eye. Better to enter life maimed than to keep both and walk into the trash-fire valley south of Jerusalem with a complete body.
  • ☩Paul takes one look at Samson and writes one sentence to a church that thought it was fine: "Let anyone who thinks he stands watch out that he does not fall."
  • ☩The Bible's surgical metaphors are not cruelty. They are the math of a Savior who would rather have part of us forever than all of us thrown away.
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday Samson told Delilah everything, and we saw the slow downhill of a man whose precepts had quietly become preferences. The Hebrew verb at the head of his fall was ra'ah, "he saw." Today Jesus picks up that very verb in Greek and presses it harder. "Everyone who looks at a woman lustfully has already committed adultery with her in his heart." The seeing-verb that opened Samson's story is the seeing-verb Jesus is naming as a heart-act before the body ever moves. And then He hands us a prescription so blunt only one word fits: "extreme." If your eye causes you to sin, pluck it out. The Word of God is not interested in cosmetic upgrades to our spiritual life today. It is interested in removing what is killing us.

🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
The Valley South of the Walls

When Jesus warns about being thrown into "hell" in Mark 9, the English word covers a Greek word that covers a specific place on a map. Gehenna is the Greek transliteration of the Hebrew ge ben-hinnom, "valley of the son of Hinnom," a ravine running along the southern and southwestern walls of Jerusalem. Every person in Jesus's audience knew which valley He meant because they had walked past it. In the days of kings Ahaz and Manasseh that valley was where Israelite parents burned their own children alive as offerings to the Canaanite god Molech, a horror the prophet Jeremiah named explicitly (Jer. 7:31, 19:2-6). King Josiah's reforms eventually defiled the place so it could never be used for that worship again, and by the first century the valley had become the city's continuous garbage fire, where refuse, animal carcasses, and the bodies of executed criminals were thrown into a smoke that never lifted off the southern ridge.

When Jesus closes Mark 9:48 with the phrase "where their worm does not die, and the fire is not quenched," He is not making it up. He is quoting the last verse of Isaiah (Isaiah 66:24), the prophet's image for the final fate of those who refused God. The first-century listener heard three things at once. A real valley they could point to. A historical horror their grandparents had paid for. And an Isaiah text the synagogue had read on countless Sabbaths. Jesus is not invoking a generic afterlife concept. He is naming the trash-fire valley south of their hometown and telling them not to walk into it carrying everything they currently insist on carrying.

📖 Into the Word

Six strongholds sit across the path this morning (self-reliance, public piety, lust, judgmentalism, hatred of enemies, anger without cause), and Jesus walks straight into Mark 9 where He names the only kind of action serious enough to take them out. His language is surgical. The verb under "causes you to sin" is the verb the surgeon uses on something that is trapping the patient. The math He offers (better to enter life maimed than to be thrown whole into the fire) is the math of a doctor who has already counted the cost of leaving the tumor alone.

Mark 9:42-48
☩ Open in Bible App
"But whoever causes one of these little ones who believe in me to fall away, it would be better for him if a heavy millstone were hung around his neck and he were thrown into the sea. And if your hand causes you to fall away, cut it off. It is better for you to enter life maimed than to have two hands and go to hell, the unquenchable fire. And if your foot causes you to fall away, cut it off. It is better for you to enter life lame than to have two feet and be thrown into hell. And if your eye causes you to fall away, gouge it out. It is better for you to enter the kingdom of God with one eye than to have two eyes and be thrown into hell, where their worm does not die, and the fire is not quenched."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

The Greek verb behind "causes you to fall away" is skandalizo. The English word "scandalize" comes from it, but the action behind the Greek is older and meaner. A skandalon was the trigger-stick of a hunter's trap, the small wooden piece that, when something brushed against it, snapped the snare closed. Jesus is not talking about embarrassment. He is talking about being caught. The hand, the foot, the eye are the parts of us most likely to brush the trigger. He is not saying the body is the problem (Mark 7 has already located the problem inside the heart). He is saying the body is where the trap closes, and the trap is closable.

Then the math. Three times in seven verses: better for you to enter life. The verb is straight from the Greek of John 3:16 and Romans 6:23, the eternal life Jesus came to give. The sentence is doing arithmetic between two outcomes. One ending preserves the body part and loses the person. The other ending costs the body part and keeps the person. Jesus is not endorsing self-mutilation; rabbis and church fathers from the earliest days have read this metaphorically, because Jesus's own ministry was full of physical healing rather than amputation. He is endorsing radical action against whatever is closing the trap. The first time you read it, the language sounds extreme. The second time, you realize it is the language of a Surgeon who knows what is at stake.

And the final line lands on the valley south of Jerusalem. "Where their worm does not die, and the fire is not quenched." That picture is not invented metaphor. Jesus is quoting Isaiah 66:24, lifting a phrase from the Old Testament about the final fate of unrepented rebellion and laying it on top of a valley His audience could see from the temple wall. The trash-fire south of the city was a parable in the air. He is not threatening; He is naming. The kingdom is not a vague invitation, and the alternative to it is not an abstract idea. Both are real and both are coming, and the choice is being made today, with the hand and the foot and the eye still in our possession.

Better to enter the kingdom with one of something than to enter the valley with two.

1 Corinthians 10:12
☩ Open in Bible App
"So, whoever thinks he stands must be careful not to fall."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

Paul writes this verse to a Corinthian church that was in love with its own spiritual giftings and convinced its standing was secure. He has just walked them through five chapters' worth of warning examples from Israel's wilderness, the very generation that watched the Red Sea split, ate manna for forty years, and still fell at every test in front of them. The Greek verb behind "thinks he stands" is participial: ho dokon hestanai, "the one who supposes himself to be standing." The self-assessment is the lever. Paul is not telling weak Christians to be more careful. He is telling confident ones to be most careful.

Yesterday's Samson is exhibit A. He thought he stood. He had every right to think so. He had killed a lion with his bare hands and a thousand Philistines with a donkey's jawbone. The Spirit of the LORD had rushed on him more than once. The hair on his head was a daily reminder of a vow he had not (yet) broken. And he was the most spiritually self-confident man in Israel right up to the morning his strength left him. Paul takes one look at that story and writes one sentence to a church that thought it was fine. The verses Jesus pulls out of the same Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 6:2 on the trumpet-blast of our own goodness, Matthew 7:1-2 on the judging we will be judged by, and 1 Corinthians 4:5 on letting God be the Judge) all sit in the same paragraph in Paul's brain. Confidence in our own strength is the trigger-stick on the trap.

Matthew 5:28-29
☩ Open in Bible App
"But I tell you, everyone who looks at a woman lustfully has already committed adultery with her in his heart. If your right eye causes you to sin, gouge it out and throw it away. For it is better that you lose one of the parts of your body than for your whole body to be thrown into hell."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

This is the verse the Mark 9 surgery is borrowed from. The Sermon on the Mount said it first: lust is not a body act that begins at the bedroom door, it is a heart act that has already finished by the time the eye moves. The Greek word for "looks" is blepo, the simple seeing verb, but the verb attached to it is epithymesai, "to desire intensely," from epithumia, the L3 Sunday cabinet of cravings we met in 1 John 2:16. Jesus is not condemning the first half-second of seeing; the eye is going to see what it sees. He is condemning the continued look that has crossed over into ownership.

The "right eye" is specific on purpose. In the ancient world the right side was the prized side, the eye an archer aligned with the bow, the eye a swordsman used to track a duel. Jesus is saying take out your best, your favorite, the asset you would most miss if it left, if that is the asset the enemy is using to set the trap. The Sermon then folds in everything else worth inventorying today: the unjust anger that puts us in danger of judgment (Matthew 5:22), the love-of-enemies that breaks the inner barrier we keep building against the people who hurt us (Matthew 5:44). Each one is a stronghold the enemy has been building. Each one is a thing to take out. The Surgeon is patient, but He is not cosmetic.

🎯 Street Level
The Saturday Morning Pantry Purge

Every adult who has ever tried to lose weight while keeping a stash of chips in the pantry knows how the half-measure works. You set a rule: one serving per day. You buy the smaller bag. You hide the bag behind the cereal boxes so it is not the first thing you see. You promise yourself you will not eat them after eight at night. The rules hold for ten days and then quietly come apart. Two years in, the scale has not moved, and the chip stash is still there because you have rationalized a hundred ways the stash is not actually the problem. The problem is willpower. The problem is stress. The problem is the schedule. The problem is the kids. The problem is anything except the bag on the shelf.

Then one Saturday morning, for reasons you cannot fully explain, you take a kitchen garbage bag and you walk into the pantry and you throw it all out. The chips, the cookies, the candy you bought "for the kids" but mostly ate yourself, the leftover Halloween bucket from October. The whole shelf goes into the bag, the bag goes to the bin, the bin gets wheeled to the curb. By the end of the week you have lost three pounds without trying. Not because three pounds of food left the house, but because the trigger-stick of the trap is no longer in the building. That is the kind of action Jesus was prescribing when He told us to cut off the hand. He was not asking for self-mutilation. He was asking us to stop keeping the stash on the shelf.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
The Address That Carried the Outcome

A 2025 systematic review published in Frontiers in Public Health synthesized two decades of randomized controlled trials and quasi-experimental studies on what addiction medicine technically calls "recovery housing." Recovery houses take in people leaving inpatient addiction treatment and place them, for six months or longer, into a building where the substance is simply not allowed. The review pulled together five eligible trials with participant counts ranging from a hundred and fifty to four hundred and seventy, with follow-up out to two years. The finding was unusually clean for a field that has spent fifty years arguing with itself.

The people who left rehab and went home to their old address had abstinence rates the field has been bothered by since the 1970s. The people who left rehab and walked into a recovery house had abstinence rates the field has trouble believing. In one of the longest-running of the studies, residents in Oxford House recovery housing showed sixty-six percent continuous alcohol abstinence at two years, compared to forty percent in standard therapeutic communities. In a different cohort, six-month abstinence climbed from eleven percent at baseline to sixty-eight percent after six months inside a sober living house. Length of stay was the other clean variable. Six months in the house was the threshold. Stable residents recovered. Early leavers did better than nothing, but not by much.

The mechanism is the one Jesus prescribed in Mark 9. Take the hand off. Take the foot off. Take the eye out. Not because the body is the problem (Mark 7 already located the problem inside the heart) but because the body is where the trap closes, and a building where the trigger-stick is no longer in any room closes differently than a building where it still is. Two years of measured behavior turns out to depend on whether the chip stash is on the shelf.

✝️ The Christ Connection

The hand Jesus told us to cut off is the hand He stretched out and let be nailed down. The foot He told us to cut off is the foot the Roman soldier pinned to the cross with a single iron spike. The eye He told us to gouge out closed in death on the cross while the Father turned His face away. The surgery Jesus prescribed for us was performed first on Him. He did not amputate; He absorbed. He took into His own body the trigger-stick of every trap that has ever closed on a human life, walked into the trash-fire valley south of the city in our place, and came back out of it on the third day with the worm dead and the fire quenched. That is why His prescription for us is so calm and so radical at the same time. He is not asking us to do anything He has not already done at a thousand times the cost. The stronghold the enemy built in our heart was leveled at Calvary, and the only thing left for us to do is stop guarding the rubble.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Pick one stronghold you have been describing as "just how I am." A scrolling habit, a category of media, a grudge you keep nursing, an income lie you have been telling yourself, an anger pattern at home. Today, do one decisive thing that cuts the trigger-stick. Delete the app from your phone for one week (not "limit usage," delete). Block the contact. Write the person you owe an apology to and hit send before the day ends. Tape a sticky note over the spot the eye keeps landing. The decisive cut is the action. Half-measures are how Samson's hair got long enough for Delilah's scissors.
🙏 Closing Prayer

Father, today we want to be honest about the strongholds we have stopped fighting. The compromises we no longer call by their names. The trumpet we like to sound about our own goodness. The contempt for the difficult person at work that we now describe as discernment. The look we keep allowing the eye to take. The anger we keep narrating as principle. We thought we stood. Forgive us. Teach us today the Surgeon's language. Show us the one trigger-stick You want us to cut today, not all of them at once, but the one. Give us the courage to do something decisive while the hand still has the option. And cover us in the gospel that says the hand You asked us to cut is the hand You stretched out for us at the cross. We do not amputate alone; we amputate inside the wound You already absorbed. In Jesus' name, Amen.
