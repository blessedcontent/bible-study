Tuesday, Nailed to the Cross

Colossians · Week 10, Day 4
March 3, 2026
✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR
What got nailed to the cross wasn't God's law, it was your debt. And the difference between those two things changes everything.
  • ☩Colossians 2:14 is one of the most weaponized verses in Christian debate, used to argue that God's moral law was abolished at the Cross. But a careful reading of both the Greek and the context makes clear that what was nailed to the cross was the record of our sin-debt, not the Ten Commandments.
  • ☩Paul presents two possible interpretations of what was nailed to the cross: the written charge against us (like the accusation Pilate hung over Jesus), or the ceremonial law written by Moses. In context, both interpretations point away from the moral law.
  • ☩The inward circumcision of the heart, burial in baptism, and resurrection with Christ form a three-part picture of complete conversion: old self dies, new self rises, and the debt is cancelled.
  • ☩Paul's own definition of sin as violation of the Ten Commandments (Romans 7:7) makes it logically incoherent for him to say the moral law was nailed to the cross, you cannot define sin by a law and then abolish that same law.
🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
What Romans Nailed to a Cross

Roman crucifixion was a public, deliberately humiliating execution. The Romans didn't just execute criminals, they displayed them. And one of the most striking features of a Roman crucifixion was the titulus: a wooden placard nailed above the condemned person listing the charges against them. When Jesus was crucified, Pilate's inscription read "This is Jesus of Nazareth, King of the Jews" (John 19:19, a charge, however ironically worded. For ordinary criminals, the placard would have read something more direct: "Thief," "Insurrectionist," "Murderer."

This is the image Paul is drawing on in Colossians 2:14. Every person who has sinned has a cheirographon, a handwritten record of debt, a certificate of legal indebtedness, hanging over them. It lists the charges. It demands the penalty. And at the Cross, Christ took that record and nailed it there in his own body, paying the debt it demanded. The charge against us was cancelled, not the law that defined the charge.

📖 Into the Word

Today's study is anchored in Colossians 2:11–15, one of the most debated passages in the New Testament. Paul moves through three connected ideas: the inward circumcision of the heart (v. 11), burial and resurrection in baptism (v. 12), and the cancellation of the sin-debt at the Cross (vv. 13–14). To understand what was and wasn't nailed to the cross, the study draws on the ceremonial law texts from Deuteronomy and Exodus, the inner circumcision language from Romans and Deuteronomy, and Paul's own definition of sin in Romans 7:7. The key question the passage forces is precise: did Christ abolish the law that defines sin, or did he cancel the debt that sin created?

Colossians 2:11–12
☩ Open in Bible App
"In him you were also circumcised with a circumcision not done with hands, by putting off the body of flesh, in the circumcision of Christ, having been buried with him in baptism, in which you were also raised with him through faith in the working of God, who raised him from the dead."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul opens with a deliberate contrast. The false teachers troubling Colossae may have been pushing physical circumcision as a spiritual requirement, a recurring theme in Paul's letters (Galatians 5:2–3). Paul responds: the circumcision that matters is not done with hands. It is an inward act, the cutting away of the old sinful self, what he calls "the body of flesh." This is the same "circumcision of the heart" Moses described in Deuteronomy 30:6 and Paul expounded in Romans 2:28–29. Baptism then pictures this death and resurrection visually: going under the water is burial with Christ, coming up is resurrection with him. Conversion is not a certificate or a ceremony. It is a death and a new life.
Romans 2:28–29
☩ Open in Bible App
"For a person is not a Jew who is one outwardly, and true circumcision is not something visible in the flesh. On the contrary, a person is a Jew who is one inwardly, and circumcision is of the heart, by the Spirit, not the letter. That person's praise is not from people but from God."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul's parallel passage in Romans makes explicit what Colossians implies: the outward, physical sign always pointed to an inward, spiritual reality. The false teachers in Colossae had reversed this, treating the outward sign as the substance. Paul insists that the real circumcision, the one that has always mattered to God, is the circumcision of the heart by the Spirit. Compare Deuteronomy 30:6: "The Lord your God will circumcise your heart and the hearts of your descendants, and you will love him with all your heart and all your soul so that you will live." This was always God's goal. The outward ceremony was never the point, it was a pointer.
Colossians 2:13
☩ Open in Bible App
"And when you were dead in trespasses and in the uncircumcision of your flesh, he made you alive with him and forgave us all our trespasses."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The NRSV renders this "made alive together with him, having forgiven us all our trespasses." The Colossian Gentile believers were "dead in trespasses", spiritually lifeless, still carrying the full weight of a sin-debt they could not pay. Paul's language here is striking: the forgiveness of trespasses is described as being made alive. Forgiveness is not just a legal transaction; it is resurrection. The same power that raised Christ from the dead is what makes dead sinners spiritually alive.
Colossians 2:14
☩ Open in Bible App
"He erased the certificate of debt, with its obligations, that was against us and opposed to us, and has taken it away by nailing it to the cross."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
This is the verse at the center of today's study, and one of the most frequently misread in the New Testament. Two Greek words are critical. Cheirographon means a handwritten certificate of legal debt, literally a handwritten IOU or bond of indebtedness. In the ancient world, this was a document you signed acknowledging what you owed. Dogmasin (translated "obligations" or "ordinances" in other versions) refers to legal decrees or requirements. Two interpretations have been proposed by Adventist scholars: first, the cheirographon is the list of charges leveled against us, similar to the accusation Pilate nailed above Jesus in Matthew 27:37 and John 19:19–20; second, it refers to the ceremonial law written by Moses (see Deuteronomy 31:24–26). The crucial insight: in both interpretations, what is nailed to the cross is a record: either of our charges, or of the ceremonial system. Neither interpretation points to the Ten Commandments.
Matthew 27:37
☩ Open in Bible App
"Above his head they put the charge against him in writing: This is Jesus, the King of the Jews."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The Roman titulus, the written charge nailed above the condemned, gives Paul's metaphor its concrete historical grounding. Where an ordinary criminal would have their crimes listed, Jesus had Pilate's ironic inscription. Paul's point in Colossians 2:14 is that our sin-record, which should have been nailed above our own heads as the charge against us, was instead nailed to Christ's cross. He took our cheirographon. He carried what belonged to us. See also John 19:19–20 for the parallel account.
Deuteronomy 31:24–26
☩ Open in Bible App
"When Moses finished writing down on a scroll every single word of this law, he commanded the Levites who carried the ark of the Lord's covenant: 'Take this book of the law and place it beside the ark of the covenant of the Lord your God so that it will remain there as a witness against you.'"
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
This is the second interpretive option the passage raises. Moses wrote the ceremonial law in a book and placed it beside the Ark, not inside it (the Ten Commandments were inside, see Deuteronomy 10:5). This external book of ceremonial law was a "witness against" Israel, it pointed to their obligation and failure. It is this ceremonial system, with its sacrifices, feasts, and rituals, that Paul could be saying was nailed to the cross: not abolished arbitrarily, but fulfilled completely in Christ. The shadow met the substance. The pointing finger finally touched what it had been pointing at all along.
Leviticus 12:3
☩ Open in Bible App
"On the eighth day, the flesh of his foreskin is to be circumcised."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul's reference to "circumcision made without hands" in verse 11 stands in direct contrast to this ceremonial requirement. Physical circumcision was one of the most foundational stipulations of the ceremonial law, commanded under Moses. That it was a ceremonial law is exactly Paul's point: the outward, fleshly sign has been superseded by the inward, spiritual reality. Compare Exodus 12:48, where circumcision was the requirement for participation in the Passover. The entire ceremonial access system, circumcision, Passover, feast participation, pointed forward to Christ. He is the Passover lamb. He is the true circumcision.
Colossians 2:15
☩ Open in Bible App
"He disarmed the rulers and authorities and disgraced them publicly; he triumphed over them in him."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul closes this section with a victory image drawn directly from Roman military culture. When a Roman general triumphed, he paraded his defeated enemies through the streets in chains, a public display of total conquest. Paul applies this image to the Cross: the spiritual powers and authorities that held humanity in bondage through the power of the sin-debt have been publicly disarmed and humiliated. The Cross looked like defeat. Paul says it was the greatest military victory in the history of the universe. The very instrument of execution became the throne of triumph.
Luke 2:1
☩ Open in Bible App
"In those days a decree went out from Caesar Augustus that the whole empire should be registered."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
This verse is cited to show how the Greek word dogma (translated "ordinances" in Colossians 2:14) is used elsewhere in the New Testament. Here it refers to a secular imperial decree, Caesar's census order. In Acts 17:7, the same word describes Caesar's decrees more broadly. In Acts 16:4, it refers to ecclesiastical decrees from the Jerusalem Council. The word simply means a formal authoritative ruling. Its use in Colossians 2:14 does not specify which legal decrees, that must be determined from context. And context, as context carefully shows, points to the ceremonial law, not the moral law.
Ephesians 2:14–15
☩ Open in Bible App
"For he is our peace, who made both groups one and tore down the dividing wall of hostility. In his flesh, he made of no effect the law consisting of commands and expressed in regulations, so that he might create in himself one new man from the two, resulting in peace."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
This parallel passage in Ephesians identifies the "dividing wall" that Christ demolished: the ceremonial law's system of regulations that kept Jews and Gentiles separated. The "law consisting of commands and expressed in regulations", note the word dogmasin again, refers specifically to the ritual and purity requirements that created a wall of separation between Jews and Gentiles. This is the same argument Paul is making in Colossians 2:14. The ceremonial system that enforced ethnic and ritual separation has been fulfilled and set aside. Not the moral law. Not the law: the wall. The Ten Commandments never divided Jews from Gentiles; the ceremonial purity codes did.
Romans 7:7
☩ Open in Bible App
"What should we say then? Is the law sin? Absolutely not! But I would not have known sin if it were not for the law. For example, I would not have known what it is to covet if the law had not said, 'Do not covet.'"
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
This brings us to the decisive argument. Paul defines sin as the violation of the moral law, specifically quoting the tenth commandment, "Do not covet." If the moral law was nailed to the cross and abolished, then sin has no definition. You cannot transgress a law that no longer exists. Paul himself says the law defines sin. He could not, without complete self-contradiction, then say that same law was done away at the Cross. The sin-debt was cancelled. The law that defined the debt remains. This is the precise and careful distinction Colossians 2:14 is making, and the one that gets flattened whenever the verse is used to argue against the ongoing validity of the moral law.
🎯 Street Level
The Bill Was Paid. The Law Didn't Change.

Imagine you rack up a significant credit card debt, thousands of dollars, more than you can pay. Then a generous friend pays off the entire balance for you. The debt is gone. Cancelled. You owe nothing. But here's the question: did paying off your debt change the laws against fraud or theft? Of course not. The law still defines what counts as debt and what doesn't. The creditor still has the right to charge interest. What changed is that someone else absorbed the cost your violation created.

That is exactly what happened at the Cross. The cheirographon, our certificate of debt, the record of every time we broke God's moral law, was nailed there and cancelled by Christ's death. The bill was paid in full. But the law that defined the bill? It didn't change. You can't pay off a debt that doesn't exist. The law still stands, which is exactly why the debt was real, and exactly why it required such an extraordinary payment to cancel it.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
When the Record Is Wiped Clean

Americans are currently carrying over $1.21 trillion in credit card debt, a record high according to the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, with the average cardholder holding roughly $6,500 in balances at interest rates above 21%. For millions of people, that debt isn't theoretical: it's a number on a screen that follows them everywhere, accumulating daily, defining their financial freedom, and creating a ceiling they can't seem to break through. The IRS Fresh Start program, debt settlement companies, and bankruptcy proceedings all share one goal: cancelling the record of what is owed so the person can actually begin again.

Paul's image in Colossians 2:14 speaks to something even more fundamental than financial debt. Every person carries a cheirographon: a record of moral debt accumulated through a lifetime of choices that violated what they knew to be right. Unlike financial debt, this record cannot be negotiated down, settled at a discount, or discharged through bankruptcy. There is no Fresh Start program for sin-debt. There is one exception. Christ took the record, nailed it to his cross, and paid the full balance. Not a settlement. Not a partial forgiveness. Paid in full. The account is zeroed out. That is what the Cross accomplished.

✝️ The Christ Connection

The Cross is where the sin-debt of humanity was fully and finally cancelled. Not reduced. Not deferred. Cancelled. The cheirographon: the written record of every charge against every person who has ever lived, was nailed to that cross in the body of Jesus Christ. He took the titulus that should have named our crimes and bore it himself. "This is Jesus, King of the Jews" replaced what should have read: the full accounting of human failure.

But notice what this means for the law: it had to remain intact for the debt to be real. A cancelled debt proves the law still stands. Otherwise there was nothing to cancel. The moral law is not the enemy of the gospel; it is the very thing that makes the gospel necessary and the Cross comprehensible. Without the law's definition of sin, the Cross is just a tragedy. With it, the Cross is the most precise, costly, and complete act of love in the history of creation. Christ did not come to lower the standard. He came to meet it, fully, on our behalf.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Think of one specific thing from your past that you still carry: a failure, a choice, a season you're not proud of. That is your cheirographon. Today, deliberately picture it nailed to the cross. Not minimized, not explained away, not still hanging over you, nailed there, cancelled, paid in full. Then ask: am I actually living like that debt is gone, or am I still trying to pay something Christ already paid?
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday's study showed that the only way to walk in Christ is the same way you received him, by staying rooted in the Person, not supplementing him with systems or philosophies. Today Paul shows us the cost of that rootedness: it required the Cross. The reason you are free to be simply, completely rooted in Christ is that the record of everything that disqualified you has been cancelled. The debt that should have kept you out was paid. You are not just welcomed in Christ, you are welcomed because the charge against you no longer exists.

The false teachers in Colossae were adding requirements on top of what Christ had done. Today's passage reveals how deeply insulting that addition actually is: it implies the Cross wasn't enough to fully cancel the debt, that something else is still required. Paul's answer is the nailed certificate. The record is gone. Nothing remains to supplement.

🙏 Closing Prayer

Father, I confess that I often live as if the debt is still outstanding, as if I still owe something, as if the record is still there. Today I want to actually believe what Paul is saying: that my cheirographon was nailed to the cross, cancelled, taken out of the way. Not reduced. Not deferred. Gone. Help me live in the freedom of a zeroed-out account. And help me understand that your law still stands: not as a threat, but as the very thing that makes what Jesus did so costly, so precise, and so extraordinary. Because he didn't lower the standard. He met it. Fully. For me. In Jesus' name, Amen.
