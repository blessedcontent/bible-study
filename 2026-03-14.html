Sabbath, Living With Each Other

Colossians · Week 12, Day 1
March 14, 2026
✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR

Paul moves from the inner life of the believer to the closest relationships on earth: spouse, child, worker, neighbor. The new self he described last week now has to live with actual people.

  • ☩ This week covers Colossians 3:18 through 4:6, where Paul applies the theology of the new life to marriage, parenting, work, prayer, and everyday speech.
  • ☩ The core tension: it is one thing to "put on" compassion and love in the abstract; it is another to do it with the people who know you best and see you at your worst.
  • ☩ Paul's household instructions were revolutionary in context, introducing mutual obligation and accountability into relationships that Roman culture built on raw power.
  • ☩ The week closes with Paul's vision for how believers engage the world beyond the household: through persistent prayer, wise conduct, and gracious, purposeful speech.
🔁 Connection to Last Week

Last week Paul gave us the complete interior blueprint for the new life: what to aim for (things above), what to kill (earthly vices), what to strip off (the old self), what to put on (compassion, kindness, humility, love), and how to sustain it all (peace as the umpire, the word dwelling richly, everything done in Jesus' name). This week he tests the blueprint where it matters most. If the peace of Christ really rules your heart, how does that look when your spouse disagrees with you? If you do everything in the name of the Lord Jesus, what does that mean for how you treat the person who works under you, or the stranger who asks a hard question? Paul moves from theology to the kitchen table, the workplace, and the front door.

🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
The Roman Household Was a Power Structure

In the first-century Roman world, the paterfamilias held near-absolute authority. He could legally discipline his wife, sell his children into servitude, and punish his slaves without oversight. The household was not a partnership; it was a hierarchy enforced by law. Philosophers like Aristotle had long argued that this was simply the natural order: some people are born to rule, others to obey. By the time Paul wrote to the Colossians, Roman society had softened some of the harshest edges, but the basic architecture remained. Authority flowed one direction: downward.

When Paul addresses wives, children, and slaves in the same letter where he addresses husbands, fathers, and masters, he is not simply reinforcing the existing order. He is doing something no Roman moral philosopher would do: he is placing obligations on the powerful. Husbands must love, not dominate. Fathers must not provoke. Masters must deal justly, remembering that they too serve a Master. This week we will watch Paul thread a needle that neither the Romans nor the Colossians saw coming: a household built not on power, but on mutual accountability before Christ.

📖 This Week's Memory Text
Colossians 4:6
☩ Open in Bible App
"Let your speech always be with grace, seasoned with salt, that you may know how you ought to answer each one."
New King James Version · NKJV
"Let your speech always be gracious, seasoned with salt, so that you may know how you should answer each person."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
This is the official memory text for the week, quoted in the NKJV translation. It sits at the very end of Paul's practical instructions, functioning as a capstone for everything he has said about household relationships, work, and prayer. Notice the scope: "always" and "each person." Paul is not describing a communication technique reserved for special occasions. He envisions every word a believer speaks, to every person they encounter, filtered through grace and seasoned with salt. Salt preserves, purifies, and adds flavor. Gracious speech that is also "salty" is not bland politeness; it is truth delivered with wisdom and care. The fact that Paul ends his household code with a verse about how we talk says everything about what he thinks holds relationships together.

✝️ The Christ Connection

Here is the question this week will answer: what does it look like when Christ moves into a household? Not as a guest, but as Lord. Paul has already told us that Christ is supreme over all creation, that in Him all things hold together, and that we have been raised with Him into new life. Now comes the test. If Christ is truly Lord, His lordship must show up in how a husband speaks to his wife at the end of a long day, in how a father responds when his child fails, in how a worker handles a task nobody will notice. This week, we will see that the gospel is not an idea that stays in your head; it is a Person who reshapes every relationship you have.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Before Sunday's study, identify the one relationship in your life where you find it hardest to "do everything in the name of the Lord Jesus." Write down the name. This week, as Paul walks through household relationships, pay attention to where his words land closest to that name.
🙏 Opening Prayer for the Week

Father, we spent last week learning to put on the new self, and now you are asking us to wear it into our closest relationships. That is where the fabric gets tested. Open our eyes this week to see what your word has to say about the people we share our lives with, the ones who see us before the coffee kicks in and after the patience runs out. Show us what it looks like when Christ is Lord not just of our theology but of our kitchen tables, our workplaces, and our conversations. We are ready to be uncomfortable if it means being transformed. In Jesus' name, Amen.
