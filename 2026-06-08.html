Be Made Well

Growing in a Relationship with God Lesson 11, Setbacks Part 3 of 7
June 8, 2026
✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR

A woman who has bled for twelve years, spent every coin on doctors, and only gotten worse decides her last hope is the hem of a stranger's robe. She fights through a packed crowd to touch it, and the moment she does, twelve years end. Then Jesus stops everything to find her, not to scold the woman who touched Him uninvited, but to call her "daughter" in front of the whole street. The lesson of a long setback is not that God rewards the strong. It is that a worn-out faith reaching for the edge of His robe is still faith, and it is enough.

🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday a boatful of men had Jesus a few feet away and still panicked, sure His sleep meant He had stopped caring. Today Jesus steps back onto shore, and a woman who cannot even get His attention does the opposite. She has every reason the disciples had to believe heaven had written her off, twelve years of it, and instead of accusing Him she crawls toward Him through a crowd. Same Lord, same week, two very different responses to a setback that would not end. Yesterday's storm lasted an evening. Hers has lasted twelve years.

🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
Twelve Years of Being Untouchable

Under the law of Leviticus, a woman with an ongoing flow of blood was ceremonially unclean, and so was anything and anyone she touched. Her chair, her bed, the hand of a friend who steadied her, all of it became unclean until evening. For most women this meant a few days a month. For this woman it had meant twelve years without end. Twelve years barred from the temple courts. Twelve years in which a husband, if she still had one, could not share a bed or a meal with her without becoming unclean himself. The bleeding was the medical problem. The isolation was the deeper wound.

Now picture what she actually does. She pushes into a crowd packed tight enough to press against Jesus on every side, and every shoulder she brushes, every arm she squeezes past, she is technically making unclean. She has no right to be in that crowd at all, and she knows it. So she does not ask. She comes from behind, low and hidden, reaching for the part of His clothing she can grab without being seen, the tassel at the hem. She is not being timid. She is being a woman who has learned over twelve years exactly how unwelcome her touch is, and who has decided to risk the shame anyway.

📖 Into the Word

Mark sets her story inside another emergency. Jesus is already on His way to a dying girl, with the girl's father, a synagogue leader named Jairus, pulling Him forward and a crowd crushing in on every side. The woman has to interrupt the most important errand on the street just to reach the hem. Watch how Jesus treats the interruption.

Having heard about Jesus, she came up behind him in the crowd and touched his clothing. For she said, 'If I just touch his clothes, I'll be made well.' Instantly her flow of blood ceased, and she sensed in her body that she was healed of her affliction. Immediately Jesus realized that power had gone out from him. He turned around in the crowd and said, 'Who touched my clothes?' His disciples said to him, 'You see the crowd pressing against you, and yet you say, "Who touched me?"' But he was looking around to see who had done this. The woman, with fear and trembling, knowing what had happened to her, came and fell down before him and told him the whole truth. 'Daughter,' he said to her, 'your faith has saved you. Go in peace and be healed from your affliction.'"
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

A whole crowd was pressing against Jesus, shoulder to shoulder, jostling Him the entire walk. Only one touch drew anything out of Him. The difference was not the force of the grip but the faith behind it. The word translated "saved you" is the Greek sozo, and it carries both meanings at once, to be healed and to be rescued, the body mended and the whole person made safe. That is why Jesus refuses to let her slip away healed but anonymous. He stops the parade, not to shame the unclean woman who touched Him without permission, but to look her in the face and give her a name in public, "daughter." Twelve years of feeling like a contamination, ended with one family word. Her story is the memory text in flesh, the long affliction that produced an endurance that finally produced a hope brave enough to reach (Romans 5:3-5).

🎯 Street Level
The Ember Under the Ash

Anyone who has kept a wood fire going overnight knows the morning ritual. You come down to what looks like a dead firebox, cold gray ash, no flame, no glow, and your first thought is that you will have to start over from scratch with paper and kindling and a match. But you crouch down and stir the ash anyway, and there, buried at the very bottom, is a single ember the size of a pea, still faintly orange. You lay a few slivers of dry wood against it and breathe on it, slow and gentle, and within a minute the whole fire is climbing again.

Twelve years of bleeding had banked this woman's faith down to about that much. Not a roaring confidence, not a bold declaration, just one tired ember she was half ashamed of, an "if I could only touch His clothes" she barely said out loud. And it was enough. The fire never asks how big the ember was before it catches. A coal the size of a pea relights the same blaze as a coal the size of your fist. Jesus did not measure the strength of her grip. He felt the faith behind the faintest brush of a fingertip in a crowd of people shoving against Him, and He turned around to call it great. If your own faith has burned down to one weak ember under years of ash, you do not have to start the fire over. You only have to stir it and reach. He does the catching.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
The Wound Under the Wound

Doctors who treat long-term illness have started measuring something besides the disease. In a 2025 survey of 2,044 U.S. adults, researchers found that ongoing loneliness lined up with chronic pain, fatigue, depression, and insomnia more tightly than age, income, or whether a person lived alone. The isolation was not just a side effect of being sick. It had become its own illness. A separate study of stroke survivors pointed to the mechanism underneath it: what tracked most closely with their loneliness was not the disability itself but the stigma attached to it, the sense of being a burden. Statistically, that shame accounted for much of the loneliness, more than the physical limits did. The stigma was carrying a large share of the damage.

That is the wound under the wound, and it is exactly the one this woman had carried for twelve years. The bleeding was the medical problem. Being untouchable was the deeper one, every chair she sat in left unclean, every hand she might have held kept off limits, a body that turned her into a contamination instead of a person. So when Jesus stops the whole crowd to find her and calls her "daughter" in front of everyone, He is not only healing the flow of blood. He is reaching past it to the worse injury, the one twelve years of stigma had carved into her, and announcing where the whole street can hear that she belongs. He treats the loneliness as the thing that needed a name, because He always knew it was the worse illness of the two.

✝️ The Christ Connection

Everywhere else in Leviticus, contact with the unclean runs one direction. Touch the leper, the corpse, the bleeding, and their uncleanness flows into you. You go home defiled and wait until evening. When this woman grabbed His hem, the current ran backward. His wholeness flowed into her faster than her defilement could flow into Him. That is the whole gospel in a single touch. Jesus does not keep a safe distance from what is broken in us. He lets it reach Him, He absorbs it, and He sends His own cleanness back down the line. He would do it on the largest possible scale at the cross, taking every kind of uncleanness into His own body so that the people who touch Him by faith walk away made well. And to every worn-out person who comes weary and heavy-laden, He still says, come to Me, and I will give you rest (Matthew 11:28-29).

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Name the thing you have been carrying so long you have quietly stopped bringing it to God, the "twelve year" problem you assume is past hoping for. The chronic pain, the marriage, the kid, the habit you have prayed about so many times it feels pointless. Today, reach for the hem about that exact thing one more time. A short, honest, even embarrassed prayer counts. The woman's was barely more than a whisper to herself.
🙏 Closing Prayer

Jesus, some of us have a wound that will not close, and we have grown quiet about it because hoping hurts more than not hoping. We come from behind today, low, half-believing, reaching for the edge of Your robe one more time. Thank You that You do not measure the strength of the grip. Thank You that You stop the whole parade to call the unclean one "daughter," to give the cut-off one a family. Let Your wholeness run back down the line into the oldest, tiredest places in us. In Jesus' name, Amen.

What would it look like this week to reach for the hem about that exact thing again, not with a bold prayer, just an honest one?"
