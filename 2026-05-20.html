Examples of Faith

Growing in a Relationship with God Lesson 8, Having Faith Part 5 of 7
May 20, 2026
✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR

Abel, Enoch, Noah, Abraham, Sarah, Moses, Rahab, Gideon, Samson, David, the prophets, women who received their dead by resurrection, men sawn in two; by verse 32 the writer of Hebrews is running out of breath, and he finally admits, "time would fail me."

  • ☩Hebrews 11 is the family album of the early church, and every name on the wall lived by the same operating system. Not the same circumstances. Not the same results. The same response to the same God.
  • ☩The chapter's hinge is verse 6. To draw near to God you need two convictions and only two: that He is, and that He rewards those who diligently seek Him. Existence and character. Everything else builds on those two pillars.
  • ☩The same chapter says, in verse 3, that the universe itself was framed by the word of God so that what we see was made out of what we do not see. Faith is the only way to read the world correctly, all the way down to the atoms.
  • ☩The cloud is not asking us to be heroic. The cloud is asking us to be ordinary in the same direction it walked: toward a Person we cannot yet see, on a road we have been told leads home.
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday Jesus called the disciples' problem oligopistia, wrong-kind-of-faith, and held up a mustard seed to show them what the right kind looks like. Today the chapter that gives faith its fullest definition unrolls in front of us with thirty-nine other people who walked in that right kind across thirteen hundred years of biblical history. The mustard seed of yesterday becomes the genealogy of today. Every name on the wall was small in its own moment. Abel had one sentence in Genesis 4. Sarah laughed in a doorway. Rahab ran a brothel. Together they form the cloud of witnesses we are running alongside.

🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
The Cloud's Roll Call

Read the names slowly the way the first hearers would have. Abel, whose blood the writer says is still speaking from the ground thousands of years later. Enoch, who walked God-ward until the path simply ran out of earth. Noah, building a wooden ship for a hundred years on a coastline that had never seen rain. Abraham, sleeping in tents in a country deeded to him but never owned. Sarah, laughing at God in the doorway of her tent and being told she would have a son anyway. Joseph, on his deathbed, giving instructions for what to do with his bones four hundred years before the Exodus. Moses, choosing the abuse of the wrong people on purpose. Rahab, hanging a red cord out of the window of a brothel. By the time the writer of Hebrews reaches verse 32 the names are coming faster than the camera can focus: Gideon, Barak, Samson, Jephthah, David, Samuel, the prophets. Some who shut the mouths of lions, some who quenched the violence of fire, some whose dead were raised, some who were tortured and refused release. Finally, exhausted by the abundance, the writer just throws up his hands. "Time would fail me."

The chapter is built on a form the first hearers knew well. Greek rhetoric called it an encomium, a praise speech, in which the speaker mounted a series of examples to illustrate a single virtue. Hebrews crosses the form with the Jewish habit of covenant memory: the kind of long roll call recited in synagogues to remember who had walked this way before. The pastoral purpose was specific. The first audience was Jewish believers under quiet pressure to step back into safer religion. The writer's strategy was not to argue them into staying. The strategy was to walk them down a hallway of family portraits and let the cumulative weight of the witness do the work no argument could do. Their grandparents had lived this way. So had Sarah, and Rahab, and David. They were not strangers asking strangers to join them. They were family in the next room, and the door was open.

📖 Into the Word

Hebrews 11 is the chapter every other chapter on faith in the Bible borrows from. It opens with a definition we already met on Sabbath in Hebrews 11:1, the title deed and the courtroom evidence. It moves immediately to creation itself, then plants the chapter's hinge in verse 6 (without faith it is impossible to please God), and then rolls forty more verses of names. Today's reading lives inside the chapter from end to end. The three verse-blocks below (verses 6, 3, and the New Testament's tightest summary of the same posture in 2 Corinthians 5:7) walk us through the chapter's argument and then hand us a sentence we can carry into Thursday.

Hebrews 11:6
☩ Open in Bible App
"Now without faith it is impossible to please God, since the one who draws near to him must believe that he exists and that he rewards those who seek him."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

This is the most quoted verse in the chapter and arguably the most weight-bearing. The writer's claim is double. Without faith it is impossible to please God. And: the one who draws near to Him must believe two things. He exists. He rewards those who seek Him. Existence and character. That is the bare-minimum content of faith in the Bible's grammar. Not seventeen doctrinal positions. Not perfect theology. Two convictions. He is real. He responds to people who go looking for Him.

The Greek participle behind "the one who draws near" is proserchomenon, and we have seen it before. Last week's Thursday on prayer used it in Hebrews 11:6 as the altar-approach word, the verb used in the Greek Old Testament for the priest stepping toward the altar in the temple. Hebrews 11:6 is not describing casual curiosity about God. It is describing approach. Drawing near. The kind of move you make when you have decided you are not just thinking about Him; you are walking toward Him. And the writer says you cannot make that move without the two convictions in your pocket. If He is not real, you are walking toward nothing. If He does not reward seekers, you are walking toward Someone indifferent. The chapter's heroes had both pockets full.

The word translated "rewards" is misthapodotes, a long Greek compound that literally means "payer-back of wages." The picture is a fair employer paying out at the end of the day. Hebrews uses it to picture God as the one who keeps an exact record and pays everything He has promised. Notice what the verse does not say. It does not say He rewards those who feel a lot or those who reason their way to certainty. It says He rewards those who seek. The verb is active. The reward is for the people who keep walking toward Him, like Abraham at seventy-five leaving Ur on a verbal promise, like Sarah at ninety opening her tent door to receive a son. The whole chapter is a list of people who took this verse at face value before the verse was written.

Notice also how this connects with what we have already seen this week. Matthew 17:20's mustard seed becomes legible here. Faith does not need to be massive; it needs to be aimed at the right Person with the right two convictions in hand. Mark 9:24's father is a verse-6 case study. He had both convictions, mixed with honest unbelief, and brought them to Jesus anyway. The verse does not require purity. It requires direction.

Hebrews 11:3
☩ Open in Bible App
"By faith we understand that the universe was created by the word of God, so that what is seen was made from things that are not visible."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

Before the chapter lists a single human example, the writer plants a claim about reading the world. The universe was framed by the word of God. Every atom of what we see was made from what we cannot see, namely, the spoken word and the prior thought of the Creator. This is faith working at its most foundational layer, beneath the level of any specific prayer or any specific decision. To say that anything exists is, in the Bible's grammar, already a faith move.

The Greek verb behind "understand" is nooumen, the verb of mental perception, the same root that gives us "noetic" in English. The writer is not saying faith is the absence of reason. The writer is saying faith is the form of reason that gets the picture right when the picture includes God. Romans 10:17 works on the same wavelength: faith comes by hearing, and hearing by the word of Christ. The Word that called the worlds into being is the Word that calls faith into being in us. The universe and the believer are made the same way, by the speech of God to silence, calling something where there was nothing.

This verse also presses on the modern objection that faith is the opposite of science. The chapter says the opposite. Faith is what makes coherent the very enterprise of looking at the world and expecting it to make sense. The Person whose word holds atoms together is the Person whose word holds Hebrews 11 together. The witnesses on the wall did not need a microscope to know that the molecules of their tents and their bread and their bones had been spoken into being by the same Voice they were trusting on the road. They simply read both kinds of evidence with the same eyes.

2 Corinthians 5:7
☩ Open in Bible App
"For we walk by faith, not by sight."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

Five English words, two Greek prepositional phrases, one of the most quoted lines in the New Testament. Paul writes this from the middle of a passage about why we keep going as believers even when our outer self is wasting away. The Greek behind "by faith" is dia pisteos; "by sight" is dia eidous. The first is the kind of seeing we have already met all week (the title deed, the cloud of witnesses, the marketplace loyalty); the second is the kind of seeing that requires the eye to confirm the data on the spot. Paul's claim is that the believer's normal mode of locomotion is the first, not the second.

This sentence is the bridge from Hebrews 11:7-40's long roll call to our week. Every name in the cloud walked this way. Noah walked by faith for a hundred years before a drop fell. Abraham walked by faith from Ur to Canaan and never owned the land in his lifetime. Moses walked by faith out of an Egyptian palace into a wilderness. The prophets walked by faith into prisons and out of caves. And we are not asked to be more than they were. We are asked to walk in the same direction, on a shorter road, with a more visible Cross at the front of the line. Philippians 2:12-16 says to work out our salvation with fear and trembling, the way a person walks by faith on a path they have not yet seen the end of. The five virgins in Matthew 25:8 who tried to borrow oil at midnight discovered the one thing you cannot borrow: someone else's walk. And Luke 17:5 is the disciples' honest prayer at the start of every such walk: "Lord, increase our faith." Today's verse hands us the verb that can carry that prayer. Walk. By faith. Not by sight.

🎯 Street Level
Three Names on the Same Hook

A nineteen-year-old shows up for his first overnight shift at the volunteer fire department in a small town in eastern Kentucky. The bay is quiet at midnight. He walks past the row of turnout gear hanging on numbered hooks. The hooks have names taped above them in faded label tape, and the one he stops in front of has his last name on it three times in a row. His grandfather wore that gear in the 1980s. His uncle wore it in the 1990s. The current owner is his second cousin, on the night-call list this week, asleep on the cot down the hall. The roster on the wall by the door starts in 1962, and his great-grandfather is the third entry from the top. The kid has not even gotten his own boots out of the box yet. But every move he is going to make for the rest of his career is going to be cushioned by the muscle memory of men he never met, who taught the men who taught him. He is not starting from zero. He is joining a relay that has been running through this firehouse for sixty years.

Hebrews 11 is a roster on the wall. Abel three entries from the top of the human story; Enoch a few down; Noah, Abraham, Sarah, Moses, Rahab, Gideon, David, the prophets, the women who received their dead by resurrection. We are not the first to put our hand on the hook with our name on it. The faith we are exercising this morning, however small it feels, is being exercised inside a relay that goes back through Jeremiah's cell and the wilderness wanderings and the door of Sarah's tent. The witnesses on the wall did not have an easier road than ours. Several of them died without seeing what they had been promised. What they had was the LORD's word and the hook with their name on it, and that turned out to be enough. We have the same word and the same hook. The cloud is not telling us to be heroic. The cloud is telling us we are already family.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
The Twenty Questions That Predicted Resilience

In 2001, two psychologists at Emory University, Marshall Duke and Robyn Fivush, gave a twenty-question test to forty-eight families. The questions were not about academic ability or income. They were about family history. Do you know how your parents met? Do you know where your grandmother grew up? Do you know an illness your family went through and got past? The researchers also gave the children a full battery of psychological tests. The twenty-question scale turned out to be the single best predictor in the battery of self-esteem, of lower anxiety, of a stronger sense of control over the child's own life. The September 11 attacks happened two months into the study, and the researchers could follow how the same children handled a national trauma. Kids who knew their family's stories of getting through hard things came out the other side measurably more grounded than kids who did not. The 2025 review of the field in the journal Parenting, co-authored by Fivush, lists this work as foundational and reports that two decades of follow-up studies have continued to replicate the finding. Family stories, told in coherent and emotionally honest ways, are among the strongest known protective factors in adolescence. The cloud of witnesses is not a sentimental ornament. It is a working psychological structure.

Hebrews 11 is twenty questions on a much longer scale. Do you know who left Ur without a forwarding address? Do you know who built the ark before the rain? Do you know the shepherd who would not wear Saul's armor? Do you know the women who received their dead by resurrection? The writer of Hebrews knew the believers he was writing to were about to walk through something hard. So he sat down at the kitchen table and told them their family stories. He told them the LORD's track record in this family. When the chapter ended, the readers were supposed to feel the way a child does after dinner with the grandparents who finally explained where the cracked picture frame came from: they were not alone, they were not the first, and the people in the pictures had made it through.

✝️ The Christ Connection

Hebrews 11 ends on a strange note in verses 39 and 40. All these people, the writer says, were commended for their faith, but did not receive what was promised, because God had provided something better for us, so that they would not be made complete apart from us. The cloud is not complete without us. And the One who completes them and us walks into the very next chapter. Hebrews 12 opens with the same word that closed chapter 11: faith. "Looking unto Jesus, the founder and perfecter of our faith." The cloud of chapter 11 is the bleachers; chapter 12 is the racetrack; and the Person we are running toward is the One who already ran the route. He went out by faith, like Abraham. He left the throne, like Moses left the palace. He walked into the suffering and refused the easier path, like the prophets. And at the cross He carried the trust the rest of us could not generate, then gave it back to us as a gift. The reason the cloud is not complete without us is that the cloud was always going to be a family, gathered around one Center, finished by the One who finished His own race so that we could finish ours.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Read Hebrews 11 out loud today, top to bottom, in one sitting. Set a timer; it takes about eight minutes. Do not stop to look up cross-references. Do not pause to take notes. Just let the names roll past you like the family album they are. When you reach verse 32 and the writer runs out of breath, slow down. When you reach the last verse, sit in the silence for a moment, and then ask one short question out loud: "Father, what is the next step of my walk by faith today?" Write whatever comes to mind on the back of your Memory Text card. Take the answer with you into Thursday.
🙏 Closing Prayer

Father, we thank You for the hallway. We thank You that we are not the first to walk this way and that the cloud is real. We thank You for Abel and his still-speaking blood, for Sarah and her doorway laugh, for Moses and his deliberate exile, for the prophets and their lonely caves, and for the names too many to list. Teach us this morning that the same Word who framed the universe is the Word who frames our small days. Show us, the way You showed them, that we do not need to see the road end in order to take the next step. Make us people who draw near because we believe You exist and that You reward those who diligently seek You. Forgive us where we have asked for sight before trust, and where we have wondered whether we belonged in the company of the cloud. Remind us that the cloud is family. Set our eyes on the One who finished His own race ahead of us. We walk by faith and not by sight. In Jesus' name, Amen.
