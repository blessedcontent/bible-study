Jesus Sees Our Faith

Growing in a Relationship with God Lesson 8, Having Faith Part 3 of 7
May 18, 2026
✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR

A Canaanite mother on the road outside Tyre talks back to the Son of God with one of the cleverest comebacks in the gospels, and the One whom no Pharisee had ever surprised marvels and says, "Great is your faith."

  • ☩Jesus reads faith. He sees it more clearly than the person who has it, and He finds it in places no first-century rabbi would have thought to look: a Roman officer's house in a garrison town, a Canaanite mother in pagan territory, a father with tears in his eyes admitting his own unbelief.
  • ☩The same chapter that records Jesus saying "I have not found such great faith in Israel" also records Him asking His own disciples in a midnight storm, "Where is your faith?" Insiders are not automatic; outsiders are not impossible.
  • ☩The most honest prayer in the gospels is six words long: "I do believe; help my unbelief!" Jesus did not turn the man away for the honesty. He healed the son.
  • ☩If Jesus could read the centurion's faith through a messenger and the Canaanite mother's faith through a comeback, He can read ours through whatever fragment we are bringing Him this morning. He sees what we cannot see in ourselves.
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday the Pharisees stood on the shore at Dalmanutha with their pre-approved menu of signs, and Jesus sighed and refused. Today the same Jesus walks twenty miles north and finds faith in the last place a rabbi would have looked for it: a Gentile woman in pagan country with a daughter at home in torment, who will not be brushed off. Same Jesus, opposite gallery. He turned away the religious experts who had access to everything and praised the Canaanite mother who had access to almost nothing. The reason was not in the data they had received. The reason was in the way they had received it. Yesterday's sigh and today's marvel are coming from the same set of eyes. He sees what no one else in the room is seeing.

🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
A Garrison Town and the Road to Tyre

Walk into Capernaum on any ordinary morning in the year Jesus lived there and you would see a Roman barracks at one end of the town, a customs post next to it, and a market street running between the two. Somewhere in the middle of it all was a Gentile officer who had quietly arranged his life around the God of Israel for years before he ever sent a messenger to Jesus. He commanded roughly eighty to a hundred soldiers and was responsible for keeping the peace in the surrounding villages, the kind of career officer the imperial army built itself around: chosen for steadiness and command presence, men who did not rise by accident. The Jewish elders of Capernaum testified before Jesus that this particular centurion had paid out of his own purse to build the local synagogue. A Roman officer financing the local Jewish house of worship is the kind of detail that does not happen casually. It tells us this man had been moving in one direction for a long time, and on the day his servant fell sick, he knew exactly whose footsteps to send for.

The road north out of Galilee, through the hills toward Tyre and Sidon, ran into territory that was Phoenician and Gentile, the home country of Israel's ancestral enemies. Tyre had funded Baal worship in the days of Ahab; Jezebel was a princess of Sidon. To a first-century rabbi, this was the wrong side of the border. The woman who chases Jesus down the road in Matthew 15 is called Canaanite, the deliberate ethnic label of the people Israel had been told to drive out under Joshua. She was not just outside the synagogue; her ancestors were the synagogue's nightmare. And yet Jesus had walked deliberately into her region, twenty miles north of His normal circuit. The two surprising-faith stories that anchor today (the centurion in Capernaum and the mother on the road to Tyre) are not random Gentile cameos. They are the two countries the gospels go out of their way to walk into, and in each one the faith Jesus finds is the kind He says He has not found inside Israel.

📖 Into the Word

A mother whose daughter is at home in torment is following a Rabbi down a dirt road, refusing to stop. The disciples are getting embarrassed by her. The Rabbi is silent at first, then crisp, then finally seems to insult her with a proverb about house dogs. And the woman, instead of breaking, picks up the proverb, turns it, and hands it back to Him as a comeback so sharp the gospel writers preserved it word for word. What Jesus says next is the highest verbal praise in the gospels for anyone outside His inner circle. "Woman, great is your faith." This is the heart of today's question. What was He seeing in her that her neighbors, her ethnicity, her gender, and even her own pain were obscuring?

Matthew 15:21-28
☩ Open in Bible App
"When Jesus left there, he withdrew to the area of Tyre and Sidon. Just then a Canaanite woman from that region came and kept crying out, 'Have mercy on me, Lord, Son of David! My daughter is severely tormented by a demon.' Jesus did not say a word to her. His disciples approached him and urged him, 'Send her away because she's crying out after us.' He replied, 'I was sent only to the lost sheep of the house of Israel.' But she came, knelt before him, and said, 'Lord, help me!' He answered, 'It isn't right to take the children's bread and throw it to the dogs.' 'Yes, Lord,' she said, 'yet even the dogs eat the crumbs that fall from their masters' table.' Then Jesus replied to her, 'Woman, your faith is great. Let it be done for you as you want.' And from that moment her daughter was healed."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

The woman gets two titles in the text and they do not match. Matthew calls her a Canaanite, the loaded ancient name for the people who were supposed to be driven out under Joshua. Mark's parallel calls her a Syrophoenician, the contemporary first-century label. Matthew chose the older word on purpose. He wanted his Jewish readers to feel the impossible distance between this mother and the kingdom Jesus had come to announce. Then he wanted them to watch her cross it.

The proverb Jesus quotes back at her in verse 26 has thrown more readers off this passage than any other line in the gospels. Two details unlock it. The Greek word for "dogs" here is kynaria, the diminutive form. Not the scavenger street dog of pagan insult, but the small house dog that lives under the family table. Jesus is not calling her vermin; He is using a household image. And the woman hears the room He has just placed her inside. "Yes, Lord, yet even the dogs eat the crumbs that fall from their masters' table." She has just claimed standing inside the household. Her theological reflex is faster than the disciples'. She knows that crumbs from this Table are enough.

The phrase Jesus uses to commend her is striking. Megale sou he pistis. "Great is your faith." It is one of only two times in all four gospels Jesus says this to anyone. The other is the centurion two chapters earlier. Both recipients are Gentiles. Both are people first-century Israel would have classified as outsiders. And in both cases the faith Jesus praises is not eloquent theology; it is the refusal to let go of who He is. The centurion holds Him to His authority. This woman holds Him to His character. Each one takes Jesus at His word more readily than the disciples have done in three years of following Him.

What Jesus saw in her, the rest of the road party missed. They saw a noisy foreign woman who would not go away. He saw a faith that already knew He was the Master of the table and that one crumb of His attention would be enough for what she needed. The most disclosing line in the passage is not her comeback; it is His marveling. The Son of God, who needs no testimony from any man because He knows what is in man, the same Person who saw through the Pharisees on the shore the day before, looks at her and says aloud, "Your faith is great." He was not flattering her. He was reading her.

Luke 7:1-10
☩ Open in Bible App
"When he had concluded saying all this in the hearing of the people, he entered Capernaum. A centurion's servant, who was highly valued by him, was sick and about to die. When the centurion heard about Jesus, he sent some Jewish elders to him, requesting him to come and save the life of his servant. When they reached Jesus, they pleaded with him earnestly, saying, 'He is worthy for you to grant this, because he loves our nation and has built us a synagogue.' Jesus went with them, and when he was not far from the house, the centurion sent friends to tell him, 'Lord, don't trouble yourself, since I am not worthy to have you come under my roof. That is why I didn't even consider myself worthy to come to you. But say the word, and my servant will be healed. For I too am a man placed under authority, having soldiers under my command. I say to this one, "Go," and he goes; and to another, "Come," and he comes; and to my servant, "Do this!" and he does it.' Jesus heard this and was amazed at him, and turning to the crowd following him, he said, 'I tell you, I have not found so great a faith even in Israel.'"
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

The centurion never speaks to Jesus in person. The whole conversation happens through two sets of messengers and one set of soldiers' instincts about chain of command. He cannot bring himself to face the Rabbi directly, which the elders read as humility, and which Jesus reads as faith. The pivot point in the story is the centurion's logic. He has spent his career inside an authority structure. He gives a one-word order and a hundred men obey. He has not seen anything Jesus has done at close range. But he has inferred from rumor what kind of authority Jesus must have over the spirit world and the body's diseases, and his inference is exact. "Say the word, and my servant will be healed."

Jesus is "amazed." The Greek is thaumazo, the verb used when the disciples are stunned at His miracles and now used of His response to this man. In all four gospels, this is one of only two times the Bible says Jesus marveled. The other is in Mark 4:40, where in a different boat on a different evening He looks at His own disciples in the middle of a storm and asks, "Why are you so afraid? Do you still have no faith?" The verbs are the mirror image of each other. Outside the camp, Jesus marvels at faith. Inside the camp, He marvels at the absence of it. Today's pairing of these two stories is not accidental. It is the deliberate point. The label "religious insider" guarantees nothing. The label "Gentile outsider" forfeits nothing. The eyes that read faith are the same eyes in both rooms.

Mark 9:24
☩ Open in Bible App
"Immediately the father of the boy cried out, 'I do believe; help my unbelief!'"
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

This is the most honest prayer in the gospels, and it comes out of the mouth of a father whose son has been tormented since childhood and just had a seizure at Jesus' feet. The man has already brought the boy to the disciples and the disciples could not help him. He is running on years of disappointment and an hour of fresh humiliation. And Jesus says to him, "Everything is possible for the one who believes." The father's response is six words long in English and seven words in Greek. Pisteuo; boethei mou te apistia. "I believe; help my unbelief."

The two words at the heart of the prayer are pistis's verb (pisteuo, "I believe") and its negated form (apistia, "unbelief"). The father confesses both at the same time. He is not pretending his faith is bigger than it is, and he is not pretending it does not exist at all. He is bringing the mixture honestly into the open. And Jesus does not turn him away for the honesty. He casts the demon out and gives the son back to him, alive. The verse that records this exchange in the gospel of John, John 2:23-25, says Jesus would not entrust Himself to people who believed in His miracles superficially "because He knew what was in man." He sees through performance. But He also sees through honesty. The faith He could read in the centurion at a distance, in the Canaanite mother in pagan country, and in this father in front of a screaming boy, was a faith that did not pretend. He reads ours the same way.

🎯 Street Level
The Audition She Heard at Lunch

A high school drama teacher in her fifteenth year of casting spring plays is sitting in the cafeteria during her duty period, half-grading. A ninth-grader she barely knows is reading at the table closest to the trash cans, away from his loud friends. He is moving his lips around the words. She watches a little longer and realizes he is not just sounding them out. He is shaping them. There is meter in the way he is reading the page, and his eyebrows are doing something small and exact. On audition day three weeks later he comes in for one of the smaller chorus parts because somebody told him he should at least try. She listens to him read his three lines, then hands him the script for the lead. "I want you to read this for me." He stumbles for a line and a half, then settles in and reads the part the way she had heard him reading under his breath at lunch. By the second page she has stopped writing and is just listening. She has seen something he has not begun to see in himself.

That is the eye Jesus had on the centurion through a messenger, on the Canaanite mother through a comeback, and on the father with tears in his eyes asking Him to help his unbelief. None of the three knew what He was reading in them. The centurion thought he was sending a respectful note about authority. The mother thought she was making one last desperate argument. The father thought he was admitting a half-faith he was ashamed of. Each one was actually showing Him the most disclosing thing about themselves at that hour, and He saw it before they did. The morning Jesus says "great is your faith" over us is the morning we discover what our small, real, unedited prayer just put in front of His eyes.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
What the Trained Eye Reads in Skin

In November 2024, JAMA Dermatology published a systematic review and meta-analysis of one hundred studies asking who actually catches skin cancer when. The numbers landed in clean rows. When the lesion is a suspicious mole and the doctor is using dermoscopy (a handheld lens that magnifies the skin and reveals features invisible to the unaided eye), experienced dermatologists hit 85.7 percent sensitivity. Primary care physicians using the same tool hit 49.5 percent. Same tool. Same skin. Same disease. A thirty-six-point gap in catching the thing that was actually there. The reviewers ran the comparison thirteen ways and the differential held: experienced dermatologists had 13.3-fold higher odds of accurately diagnosing melanoma than primary care physicians. The vocabulary the trained eye reads in those first seconds is not in the lay vocabulary at all: pigment network irregularity, atypical globules, blue-white veil, asymmetry of structure. A general practitioner, the relatively untrained eye, sees a brown spot. The dermatologist's eye has already read three or four clinical features the carrier of the mole has never heard named. The disease is in the image either way. The reader is the difference.

That is the eye Jesus had on the centurion through a messenger, the Canaanite mother through a comeback, and the father on the mountain road. The centurion did not name what he was showing. He thought he was sending a respectful note about chains of command. Jesus read three clinical features inside that note and marveled out loud. The Canaanite mother thought she was making a desperate last argument. Jesus read great faith. The father at the foot of the mountain thought he was confessing a half-belief he was ashamed of. Jesus read the seed of trust that the trembling sentence was actually showing Him. None of the three carried a name for what He saw. The trained Eye named it for them. And the same Person stands at our shoulder this morning. The morning we feel least sure we have anything to bring is the morning He is most likely to call it by its right name.

✝️ The Christ Connection

The Jesus who could read the centurion's faith through a messenger and the Canaanite mother's faith through a comeback is the same Jesus who, on a wooden beam outside Jerusalem, would look down and see the faith of a thief no rabbi would have approached. The thief had no track record, no theology, no doctrinal training, and no time. He had a turning, a confession, and a single request: "Jesus, remember me when You come into Your kingdom." And the same eyes that read the Canaanite mother on the road to Tyre read the thief on the next cross and answered, "Today you will be with Me in paradise." This is the qualification of the Judge to whom we are bringing our faith. He is not impressed by the size of our claim and He is not confused by the noise around our doubt. He sees through it. He saw a kingdom in the thief's three sentences and He praised His own Father in heaven for revealing things to babes that had been hidden from the wise. When we come to Him this morning unsure how much faith we have, the question is not how much. The question is whose. The eyes reading us are the eyes of the One who reads accurately.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Pray today the most honest six-word prayer in the gospels: "I believe; help my unbelief." Do it once before you leave the house and once before you go to sleep. Do not try to make your faith bigger before you pray it. Bring the exact mixture you actually have. Let Jesus do what He did for the father in Mark 9. He healed the son. He did not require the father's prayer to be tidier first.
🙏 Closing Prayer

Lord Jesus, You are the One who marveled at the centurion who never met You and the Canaanite mother who would not stop asking, and who marveled also at the absence of faith in disciples who had walked next to You for years. We confess that we measure our own faith by our feelings, our certainty, our recent record of prayers answered or not, and almost never by who You are. Read us today the way You read them. Find the seed of trust You placed in us in the first place, and call it by name. Where we are pretending to a faith we do not yet have, gently unmask the pretense. Where we have honest belief mixed with honest unbelief, like the father with the trembling son at Your feet, do not turn us away for the mixture. Help our unbelief. Honor the small grip with which we are reaching for You this morning, because we are reaching for a great Savior and the grip does not have to be perfect for the rescue to be. In Jesus' name, Amen.

"Where in your life right now is your faith mixed with honest unbelief, the way the father's was at the bottom of the mountain (a prayer you have stopped expecting to be answered, a promise of God you have quietly filed away as not-for-you, a situation in which you say you trust Him but your stomach says otherwise)? What would it look like this week to bring that exact mixture to Jesus in the words of Mark 9:24, without first cleaning it up?"
