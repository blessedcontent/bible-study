Praise, Confession, Requests, Thanksgiving

Growing in a Relationship with God Lesson 7, Practical Prayer Part 5 of 7
May 13, 2026
✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR

An old man in Babylon laid a borrowed scroll of Jeremiah on his lap, did the math on seventy years, and prayed sixteen verses that contained praise, confession, requests, and thanksgiving in one sustained pour-out.

  • ☩Daniel 9 is what the Lord's Prayer looks like at full length: God-ward worship and surrender first, us-ward needs and forgiveness next, all of it driven by what Daniel had just been reading.
  • ☩The four-part prayer model (praise, confession, requests, thanksgiving) is not a checklist; it is the natural shape of speech to a Father whom you actually know.
  • ☩The Hebrew word for "forgive" in Daniel's climactic three-fold cry is salah, a verb the Old Testament uses only of God. Humans pardon. God salahs.
  • ☩Most of our prayers are eighty percent requests and twenty percent everything else. Daniel's prayer is the inverse, and it is a model for the rebalancing.
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday Jesus gave the disciples nine plain lines on a Galilean hillside. The first three petitions turned toward God (Your name, Your kingdom, Your will), and the next three turned toward us (bread, forgiveness, deliverance). Today's four-part model has the same architecture, just stretched out: Praise turns toward who God is, Confession asks for the forgiveness Jesus made the prayer's halfway pivot, Requests bring the daily-bread-shaped needs, and Thanksgiving completes the circuit. The textbook example is not invented for this study. It is a five-hundred-year-old prayer Daniel prayed on his knees in Babylon, and it contains all four moves in one pour-out.

🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
The Old Man Reading Jeremiah

The first verse of Daniel 9 dates the prayer to the first year of Darius the Mede, the very first year after Babylon fell to Persia in 539 BC. Daniel was now in his eighties. He had been deported as a teenager during Nebuchadnezzar's first invasion of Jerusalem in 605 BC and had served as a high official under four successive monarchs. The prayer in chapter 9 begins with the smallest detail. He was reading. The Hebrew text says he "perceived in the books"; the books in question were the scrolls of Jeremiah, the prophet whose ministry had ended just before Daniel's exile began. Daniel had a personal copy of Jeremiah's prophecies in Babylon, and he was rereading the part where Jeremiah said the captivity would last seventy years.

Daniel did the math. Sixty-eight years had passed since the first deportation. The seventy-year mark was almost here. Cyrus, the Persian who had just conquered Babylon, was rumored to be sympathetic to displaced peoples returning home. The promise was about to become history, and the right response was not to wait around to see if Jeremiah was correct. The right response was to pray. So Daniel set himself to a fast of sackcloth and ashes and let the prophet's words crack open his own. The prayer that follows is steeped in the language of Deuteronomy and Jeremiah and the Psalms; an old man whose mind has been formed by Scripture can pray almost no other way. The principle hidden in the prayer's opening sentence is that the most powerful praying is often the praying that follows close reading. Daniel did not generate the prayer from his own emotional reservoir. He let the Bible put the words in his mouth.

📖 Into the Word

Jesus' nine-line prayer in Matthew 6:5-15 is the compressed version of what Daniel prays here at length. Watch how the four components weave together. The praise opens the prayer (Daniel's first move); the confession dominates its middle; the requests cluster near the end; and the thanksgiving runs underneath the whole thing as the gratitude that he is allowed to pray it at all. Read the full prayer first, then we will trace the four movements.

Daniel 9:4-19

"I prayed to the LORD my God and confessed: Ah, Lord—the great and awe-inspiring God who keeps his gracious covenant with those who love him and keep his commands—we have sinned, done wrong, acted wickedly, rebelled, and turned away from your commands and ordinances. We have not listened to your servants the prophets, who spoke in your name to our kings, leaders, ancestors, and all the people of the land. Lord, righteousness belongs to you, but this day public shame belongs to us: the men of Judah, the residents of Jerusalem, and all Israel—those who are near and those who are far, in all the countries where you have banished them because of the disloyalty they have shown toward you. LORD, public shame belongs to us, our kings, our leaders, and our ancestors, because we have sinned against you. Compassion and forgiveness belong to the Lord our God, though we have rebelled against him and have not obeyed the LORD our God by following his instructions that he set before us through his servants the prophets. All Israel has broken your law and turned away, refusing to obey you. The promised curse written in the law of Moses, the servant of God, has been poured out on us because we have sinned against him. He has carried out his words that he spoke against us and against our rulers by bringing on us so great a disaster that nothing like what has been done to Jerusalem has ever been done under all of heaven. Just as it is written in the law of Moses, all this disaster has come on us, but we have not sought the favor of the LORD our God by turning from our iniquities and paying attention to your truth. So the LORD kept the disaster in mind and brought it on us, for the LORD our God is righteous in all he has done. But we have not obeyed him. Now, Lord our God, who brought your people out of the land of Egypt with a strong hand and made your name renowned as it is this day—we have sinned, we have acted wickedly. Lord, in keeping with all your righteous acts, may your anger and wrath turn away from your city Jerusalem, your holy mountain; for because of our sins and the iniquities of our ancestors, Jerusalem and your people have become an object of ridicule to all those around us. Therefore, our God, hear the prayer and the petitions of your servant. Make your face shine on your desolate sanctuary for the Lord's sake. Listen closely, my God, and hear. Open your eyes and see our desolations and the city that bears your name. For we are not presenting our petitions before you based on our righteous acts, but based on your abundant compassion. Lord, hear! Lord, forgive! Lord, listen and act! For your own sake, my God, do not delay, because your city and your people bear your name."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

Praise. The prayer opens with two relational adjectives, "great" and "awe-inspiring," and one covenantal claim: "who keeps his gracious covenant with those who love him." Daniel does not begin by listing what is wrong. He begins by reminding himself who he is talking to. Praise in this prayer is not a flourish; it is the necessary first move that frames everything that follows. Psalm 100, with its "Enter his gates with thanksgiving and his courts with praise," does in five verses what Daniel does in the first sentence. We tell the truth about God before we tell the truth about ourselves, because the second truth only sounds right against the first.

Confession. The middle ten verses are corporate confession. Daniel uses the word "we" relentlessly: "we have sinned, done wrong, acted wickedly, rebelled, and turned away." He was a faithful man. He had not personally bowed to Nebuchadnezzar's image; he had not personally compromised in Babylon; he had been the moral exception in his generation. And yet he confessed the nation's sin in the first person plural. He stood inside the family rather than above it. By Thursday we will see Moses doing exactly the same thing on Sinai, in a register that pointed all the way to the cross.

Requests. Verses 16 to 19 turn at last to what Daniel actually wants. Even here, the request is not "let me come home." It is, "may your anger and wrath turn away from your city Jerusalem," "make your face shine on your desolate sanctuary," "open your eyes and see our desolations," "do not delay, because your city and your people bear your name." Even the request is God-ward. Daniel asks for things on the grounds that God's reputation is at stake, not Daniel's. The climactic verse 19 is three short imperatives in Hebrew: shema, salah, hakshibah va'aseh, "hear, forgive, listen and act." The middle verb is the strongest of the three. Salah is a Hebrew verb the Old Testament uses only of God. Humans can pardon, release, let go; only God can salah. Daniel calls for the divine forgiveness because he knows no human substitute is going to be enough.

Thanksgiving. Daniel does not have a separate paragraph labeled "thanksgiving," because the whole prayer is grateful that the conversation is even happening. The phrase "compassion and forgiveness belong to the Lord our God" in verse 9 is thanksgiving in the form of a confession: gratitude that the Father he is confessing to is also the Father who pardons. The thanksgiving is not at the end. It is the air the whole prayer breathes. Praise opens the door; confession walks through it; requests do their work in the room; thanksgiving is the light that has been on since before the prayer began.

James 5:16
☩ Open in Bible App
"Confess your trespasses to one another, and pray for one another, that you may be healed. The effective, fervent prayer of a righteous man avails much."
New King James Version · NKJV
"Therefore, confess your sins to one another and pray for one another, so that you may be healed. The prayer of a righteous person is very powerful in its effect."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

James writes confession out as a corporate practice. The Greek verb is exomologeo, the same word used in the New Testament for the public acknowledgment of Jesus as Lord. Confession is not an apology whispered in the dark to make the conscience feel better. It is a public movement, a saying-out-loud that takes the secret out of its hiding place. James pairs confession with intercessory prayer ("pray for one another") and ties both to healing.

The Greek phrase behind "very powerful in its effect" is polu ischuei energoumene, "much it accomplishes when energized." The same root energ- shows up in Hebrews 4:12, where the Word is described as energes, at work. The Word at work in the reader produces a prayer at work in the church, and the church's confession-and-intercession is itself a healing instrument. We do not confess in private only. Some sins need a witness, a brother or sister who hears them and prays them out into the light.

Philippians 4:6
☩ Open in Bible App
"Don't worry about anything, but in everything, through prayer and petition with thanksgiving, present your requests to God."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

Paul writes this from prison. The Greek word for "thanksgiving" is eucharistia, the word the early church already used for the Lord's Supper. Paul tells the Philippians that requests presented without thanksgiving are an incomplete prayer. The petition has to be tucked inside the gratitude. The shape he prescribes is: anxiety dropped, prayer raised, petition specific, thanksgiving woven throughout.

The Hebrew word that sits behind eucharistia is todah, the thanksgiving offering of Leviticus 7. A worshipper who had been spared something brought a todah meal to the temple, ate part of it with friends, and gave part to the priest. The point of the meal was to share the gratitude before consuming it. Paul's prayer instruction borrows that same instinct. Wrap the request in the meal. Present petitions to God the way a survivor presents a thanksgiving offering: still asking, but already grateful.

🎯 Street Level
The Wedding Toast

When a best man does the toast right, the structure is the same every time. There is a story (praise: something specific about the bride or groom, the kind of detail that says "I have known this person for twenty years"). There is an admitted vulnerability (confession: "I almost didn't write this," "I didn't know how to follow her brother last week"). There is a request to the room (lift your glasses, raise them high). There is a thanksgiving (gratitude for being asked to stand here at all, gratitude for the couple, gratitude for the people who got the bride and groom this far). When a best man does it wrong, the structure breaks. It becomes a stand-up routine. It becomes a list of past girlfriends. It becomes a request without a story, or a story without a thanks.

The reason the four-move toast works is the same reason Daniel's prayer works. Speech to important people has a natural shape. Praise opens the door. Confession walks through it honestly. Requests do their work in the room. Thanksgiving is the lift on the way out. Most of our prayers are best-man toasts with three of the moves missing. We pile up the requests and skip the story, the vulnerability, the thanks. The toast falls flat. The room shifts in its seats. The Father, who is also the Bridegroom of the church, has been waiting to hear all four.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
When a Country Kneels

Truth and Reconciliation Commissions are the modern world's closest civic parallel to Daniel 9. After mass atrocities (apartheid in South Africa, the Rwandan genocide, decades of armed conflict in Colombia, the Indian residential school system in Canada), nations have stood up bodies whose work is, in formal terms, exactly what Daniel was doing on his knees: a corporate confession in the first person plural, in the hearing of those who were harmed, with a request for the conditions of a different future.

The Colombian Truth Commission's final report, released in 2022 after listening to nearly 24,000 witnesses, was studied at length in a 2025 article in the International Journal of Transitional Justice. The article's central concern was the post-report phase. Other truth commissions had failed because the report sat on a shelf; the Colombians built an explicit "legacy strategy" to keep the corporate confession alive in schools, regional dialogues, and ongoing research. The commission's official task included "promoting victim recognition and voluntary admission of individual and collective responsibilities by the people who took part in the armed conflict." That is Daniel's "we have sinned, done wrong, acted wickedly, rebelled" translated into civic English. A nation cannot heal until it can say that sentence honestly.

The pattern in the research is consistent across very different cultures. Where the corporate "we" is spoken honestly and at scale, the next generation does measurably better. Where it is dodged, the conflict often returns under a new name. Daniel knew this five hundred years before Christ. He had not personally bowed to Nebuchadnezzar's image; he had been the moral exception in his generation. He still confessed in the first person plural, because a nation cannot kneel one citizen at a time. The praise opens the door. The confession walks through it together. Modern transitional justice keeps relearning what an old man with a scroll on his lap knew on his knees in Babylon.

✝️ The Christ Connection

The four parts of the prayer find their consummation in Jesus. He is the perfect Praise, the Son who has glorified the Father from before the foundation of the world. He is the only One who never needed to confess, and He carried the world's confession on His back to the cross. He is the answer to every Request, in whom (as Paul writes) all the promises of God are yes and amen. And He is the source of every Thanksgiving; the meal at the heart of the church is named eucharistia because gratitude is the only adequate response to a Savior who broke His own body for us. Daniel cried "Lord, hear! Lord, salah! Lord, listen and act!" four hundred years before Christ stood between us and judgment. The cross is the answer to all three imperatives, in one Person, given once for all.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Today, time your next prayer. Spend one full minute on praise (name three specific attributes of God). Spend one minute on confession (name one specific sin you have been avoiding). Spend one minute on requests (name three specific people or situations). Spend one minute on thanksgiving (name three things you usually take for granted). Most of us do not pray a four-minute prayer; we pray a four-second one and call it humility. Set a timer if you have to. Daniel's prayer was sixteen verses long because he had something to say in each of the four movements.
🙏 Closing Prayer

Father, the great and awe-inspiring God who keeps Your gracious covenant with those who love You: we praise You first, before we ask for anything. You are righteous; we have sinned. You are faithful; we have wandered. We confess that we have prayed mostly for ourselves and only seldom for Your name to be honored, and we have asked for relief from things You may have sent us to teach us. Forgive us. Salah us, the way only You can. Hear our requests today, not on the basis of our righteous acts, but on the basis of Your abundant compassion. Make Your face shine on the corners of our lives that have grown desolate. And underneath every word, receive our thanksgiving. Thank You for the Bibles in our hands, for the bread on our tables, for the people in the next room, for the cross that made all of this possible. We pour out our hearts before You today, because You are our refuge. In Jesus' name, Amen.
