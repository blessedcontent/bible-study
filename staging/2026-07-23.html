Marriage and Singleness

The Essence of Christian Life and Witness Lesson 4, Sin in the Church Part 6 of 7
July 23, 2026
✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR

A slave in Egypt is cornered day after day by his master's wife, and one afternoon she grabs his coat. Joseph leaves it in her fist and runs. A thousand years later, Paul hands a tempted church the same strategy in one word. Flee. Not negotiate, not linger, not prove how strong you are. Run. Then he opens the church's letter about marriage and answers with a design worth honoring, mutual, faithful, and unashamed. Flee what would defile the temple, and honor the home God built for desire.

🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday stacked up three facts about a believer's body. Washed clean, joined to Christ, and occupied by His Spirit. Beautiful doctrine, but every reader knows the next morning's question. What do I actually do when desire pulls against all three? Today Paul gets ruthlessly practical. His strategy has two verbs, and neither one is complicated. Flee what would burn the temple down. Honor the design God built for desire to flourish in. One verb for the ambush, one verb for the architecture. And behind both stands a church that had written Paul a letter full of real questions about marriage, singleness, and sex, proof that these conversations belong inside the family of God, not exiled from it.

🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
Some in Corinth Swore Off Marriage Entirely

Somewhere on the road between Corinth and Ephesus, a letter is traveling in a courier's bag, and it is full of the questions churches are usually too embarrassed to ask. Should married couples stop sleeping together to be more spiritual? Should the unmarried stay single forever? Is it better never to touch a woman at all? Corinth had swung between extremes its whole short life as a church. Some members had carried the city's appetite right through the church door, the scandal and the brothel logic we have spent three days on. Others recoiled so hard they landed in the opposite ditch, deciding holiness meant renouncing sex entirely, even inside marriage. If the body is spiritually worthless, both conclusions feel logical. Indulge it, or starve it.

Paul's answer refuses both ditches. He will not treat sex as a god, and he will not treat it as garbage. It is a gift, and gifts have intended homes. What is remarkable is that he answers at all. Most religious teachers of his day would never have dignified a congregation's bedroom questions with a written reply. Paul takes them one by one, unembarrassed, because a church that cannot talk about sexuality hands the subject to the loudest voices in the culture. Corinth's streets were already discipling the church's imagination. Paul intended to take the conversation back.

📖 Into the Word

The reading begins at the temple and walks into the bedroom (1 Corinthians 6:19-7:9). That order matters. Paul does not start with rules about sex. He starts with who you are, a body indwelt and purchased, and only then answers the questions Corinth mailed him about marriage and singleness. Two commands organize everything that follows. Flee, because some temptations are not for fighting. And honor, because desire was never the enemy, only desire without a home. Watch how a man who chose singleness writes about marriage with more reverence than the culture that mocked it.

1 Corinthians 6:18
☩ Open in Bible App
"Flee sexual immorality! Every other sin a person commits is outside the body, but the person who is sexually immoral sins against his own body."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

The command is the Greek word pheugo, to flee, to run for your life, and Paul writes it in a tense that means keep on fleeing, make running your standing policy. Notice what he does not say. He does not say reason with sexual temptation, or linger and prove your strength, or see how close you can stand. Scripture treats this pull differently from other battles. You do not win it toe to toe. You win it with distance. Then comes the strange-sounding reason, that other sins happen outside the body but this one sins against your own body. Other sins use the body as a tool. This one turns the body itself, the Spirit's own address, against its Owner, which is why the command sits bracketed by union with the Lord (1 Corinthians 6:17) and the temple He lives in (1 Corinthians 6:19). Fleeing is not prudishness. It is guarding a sanctuary.

1 Corinthians 6:19-7:9
☩ Open in Bible App
"Don't you know that your body is a temple of the Holy Spirit who is in you, whom you have from God? You are not your own, for you were bought at a price. So glorify God with your body. Now in response to the matters you wrote about: 'It is good for a man not to have sexual relations with a woman.' But because sexual immorality is so common, each man should have sexual relations with his own wife, and each woman should have sexual relations with her own husband. A husband should fulfill his marital duty to his wife, and likewise a wife to her husband. A wife does not have the right over her own body, but her husband does. In the same way, a husband does not have the right over his own body, but his wife does. Do not deprive one another—except when you agree for a time, to devote yourselves to prayer. Then come together again; otherwise, Satan may tempt you because of your lack of self-control. I say this as a concession, not as a command. I wish that all people were as I am. But each has his own gift from God, one person has this gift, another has that. I say to the unmarried and to widows: It is good for them if they remain as I am. But if they do not have self-control, they should marry, since it is better to marry than to burn with desire."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

Right after the temple claim, Paul opens Corinth's letter. Now in response to the matters you wrote about. The line he quotes first, that it is good for a man not to have sexual relations with a woman, is almost certainly their slogan, the ascetic faction's proposal, and Paul refuses to bless it as a blanket rule. His counsel is striking for what it assumes. Sex inside marriage is not a concession to weakness but a duty of love, given rather than withheld, and given mutually. In a world where a wife was treated as property, Paul writes that a husband's body belongs to his wife exactly as hers belongs to him. Nobody in Corinth had ever read a sentence like that. Abstinence, he says, is for agreed seasons of prayer, and then come together again, because a door left open is a door the tempter uses. Marriage is a gift. Singleness is a gift. The only wrong move is pretending you do not burn.

Keep the chapter's shape in mind before drawing sweeping conclusions. The first half answers questions about marriage (1 Corinthians 7:1-24), the second half questions about singleness (1 Corinthians 7:25-40), and all of it responds to specific situations in one anxious congregation. Paul is not writing a complete theology of marriage here, and anyone tempted to read him as anti-marriage should hear him elsewhere, where he brands teachers who forbid marriage as carriers of demonic doctrine (1 Timothy 4:1-3) and counsels young widows to marry and build households (1 Timothy 5:14). Scripture's design holds steady even when its counsel is situational. Sex is God's gift with one home, the marriage of a man and a woman, the only arrangement the Bible ever blesses with it. And talking about all of this out loud, as Paul does, is part of protecting it.

Hebrews 13:4
☩ Open in Bible App
"Marriage is to be honored by all and the marriage bed kept undefiled, because God will judge the sexually immoral and adulterers."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

One verse, two guardrails. Marriage can be dishonored by defiling it, the affair, the wandering eye, the habit that hollows out a spouse's trust. And it can be dishonored by dismissing it, filing the whole institution under expired traditions. Scripture refuses both. Honored by all, the verse says, which means the single honor it too, and the married honor the callings of the unmarried, the way Jesus and Paul, both single men, spoke of marriage with reverence. Honor here is not nostalgia. It is treating something as weight-bearing, load-worthy, holy. The marriage bed kept undefiled is not the Bible being squeamish about sex. It is the Bible being fierce about promises. God takes vows more seriously than we take feelings, and the verse ends with Him standing guard over both.

When Paul says flee, he may well be remembering a young man in Egypt. Potiphar's wife pressured Joseph day after day (Genesis 39:6-18), and the account lingers on one physical detail, repeated over and over in the telling, that he ran out of the house and left his coat behind in her hands (Genesis 39:18). Fleeing cost him. The abandoned coat became the evidence that put him in prison. But the same story calls him a man in whom the Spirit of God lived (Genesis 41:38), and in God's timing he was given a wife (Genesis 41:45) and a life his obedience had guarded, having waited for marriage in a culture that would have laughed at him for it. Joseph teaches the honest version of today's command. Running is not free. It can cost you a coat, a job, years. It is still cheaper than the temple.

🎯 Street Level
Respect Is Measured in Feet

After the storm, a power line lies across the end of the street, and it is still alive. It jumps and spits, and every few seconds it goes quiet, which is somehow worse. Notice what nobody on that street is doing. Nobody walks up to test it. Nobody stands over it to prove their nerves are steady. The bravest man on the block is the one keeping his kids the full width of the road away from it, and not one neighbor calls him a coward. Even the linemen who come to fix it, professionals with insulated gloves and twenty years on the poles, work by a number written down long before this morning, the exact feet of air that must stay between a body and that much current. And ask any crew about the old-timers. They are not the ones who finally got comfortable around the wire. They are the ones who never did.

That is Paul's flee command wearing work gloves. Some pulls do not negotiate, do not tire, and do not care how many years you have walked with God, and sexual temptation is one of them. Scripture never tells anyone to stand over that wire and prove something. It says give it the whole street. So decide your distances on a calm day, the way the safety manual does, and the ambush never gets a vote. Running is not panic when it is policy. The world may call it fear. Let it. Joseph ran out of a house in Egypt with no coat, and heaven has called it wisdom ever since. Nobody has ever won an argument with a live wire. The winners are the ones who never let it start.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
Willpower Lost the Study

Psychologists Marina Milyavskaya and Michael Inzlicht tracked 159 university students through an ordinary week, pinging their phones to ask what they were tempted by, how hard they were resisting, and how their semester goals were coming. The assumption going in was the one our culture preaches, that the students who gritted their teeth and fought temptation hardest would come out ahead. The data said the opposite. Effortful resistance predicted exhaustion, not success. The students who actually reached their goals were simply the ones who encountered fewer temptations along the way. The study could not say why their weeks were quieter, only that quieter weeks and reached goals traveled together. The finding, published in Social Psychological and Personality Science in 2017, matched an earlier study that beeped 205 adults through their daily routines and found that the people scoring highest in self-control were not winning more of these battles than everyone else. They were fighting fewer of them.

Researchers call this situation selection, choosing your environments so the showdown never starts, and one influential review argues it can be especially effective for exactly the reason you would guess, it stops the impulse before it has time to gather strength. Which means the lab has finally caught up to a slave in Egypt who left his coat and ran, and to a one-word command written to Corinth two thousand years ago. Flee is not the counsel of weak faith. It is the strategy of people honest about voltage, who move the phone out of the bedroom, book the different flight, and decide their distances on a calm day. The people who look strongest in the moment of temptation are usually the ones who arranged never to need the moment. Willpower makes a thrilling testimony. Distance makes a faithful life.

✝️ The Christ Connection

Here is the mystery underneath the whole day. Why does the Bible guard marriage so fiercely? Because marriage was never only about the couple. From the beginning it has been a living picture of something bigger, Christ and His church, the covenant behind every covenant (Ephesians 5:31-32). That is why defiling it lies about God, and honoring it tells the truth about Him. And look closely at the Bridegroom the picture points to. Joseph fled temptation, and it cost him his coat. Jesus faced a harder assignment. When the cup of our redemption stood in front of Him, He did not flee. He stayed, He obeyed, He kept covenant with an unfaithful bride at the cost of His life, faithful where every human love has flickered. Single reader, take heart from this too. The truest, fullest human who ever lived never married, and He calls the married and the unmarried into the same romance, the love of God that outlasts every wedding. Flee what lies about Him. Honor what tells His story.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Build your flee plan while you are calm, because nobody builds one mid-ambush. Name the situation where temptation reliably finds you, the app, the late hour, the person, the hotel room on the work trip. Today, make one structural change that adds distance, move the phone charger out of the bedroom, install the blocker and hand a friend the password, change the route, book the different flight. Then add one act of honor. Married, do something today that makes your spouse feel chosen. Single, guard your own boundaries and speak well of a marriage you respect.
🙏 Closing Prayer

Father, You designed desire, so we stop apologizing for having it, and we stop pretending we can manage it alone. Teach us to run when running is the obedient thing, without shame, the way Joseph ran. Show each of us the door You have already built out of our most familiar ambush. We pray for the married among us, keep their beds undefiled and their promises heavier than their moods. We pray for the single among us, honor them as You honored Your own Son's singleness, and guard them in a culture that calls patience foolish. Heal those of us carrying broken vows, our own or someone else's. And fix all our eyes on the Bridegroom who did not flee the cross, until faithfulness stops looking like deprivation and starts looking like Him. In Jesus' name, Amen.
