Wednesday, Character of the New Life

Colossians · Week 11, Day 5
March 11, 2026
✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR

Stripping off the old self was only half the job; now Paul tells the Colossians what to put on in its place, and the entire wardrobe is built from one fabric: how Christ has already treated you.

  • ☩Paul shifts from the "take off" list (anger, malice, slander) to the "put on" list: compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness, patience, forgiveness, and above all, love.
  • ☩These eight qualities are not personality traits some people are born with; they are the character of Christ himself, reproduced in believers who are united with him.
  • ☩The motivation for forgiving others is not that they deserve it, but that Christ forgave you first. The standard is his generosity, not theirs.
  • ☩Love is called "the perfect bond of unity" because it is the one quality that holds every other virtue together and makes community possible.
🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
The Language of Getting Dressed in the Ancient World

When a Roman citizen changed social roles, they changed clothes. A boy becoming a man exchanged his childhood toga praetexta for the plain white toga virilis in a public ceremony. A soldier putting on armor became, in the eyes of the empire, a different person with different obligations. Clothing was identity. To "put on" something was to assume its character publicly, visibly, irreversibly.

Paul is drawing directly on this cultural instinct when he tells the Colossians to "put on" compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness, and patience. He is not describing an internal feeling. He is describing a public identity change. You have died with Christ. You have been raised with him. Now dress like it. The old garments (rage, malice, slander, filthy language) have been stripped off. The new garments are the character of the one who raised you. And the watching world, Paul implies, will know whose household you belong to by what you are wearing.

📖 Into the Word

Today's anchor passage is Colossians 3:12-14, the positive counterpart to the "put to death" and "put away" commands in verses 5-9. Having told the Colossians what to strip off, Paul now tells them what to put on. The supporting passages (Matthew 22:14, Romans 8:33, 2 Timothy 2:10, Deuteronomy 7:6-8, 1 Peter 2:9, and 1 John 4:11-12) establish the foundation: these qualities are not self-improvement goals. They flow from election, from being God's chosen, holy, and loved people. The standard for each quality is Christ himself.

Colossians 3:12
☩ Open in Bible App
"Therefore, as the elect of God, holy and beloved, put on tender mercies, kindness, humility, meekness, longsuffering,"
New King James Version · NKJV
"Therefore, as God's chosen ones, holy and dearly loved, put on compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness, and patience,"
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Three identity markers precede the command: eklektoi (chosen, elected), hagioi (holy, set apart), and egapemenoi (loved, beloved). Paul is not saying "try to be nice." He is saying: because of who you already are in Christ, live accordingly. The Greek word for "put on" is endysasthe, the same verb used for putting on clothing. It carries the force of a deliberate, visible act. You are choosing to wear the character of Christ the way you choose to get dressed in the morning. The word for compassion, splanchna oiktirmou, literally means "bowels of mercy," the deepest gut-level empathy. Kindness (chrestotes) describes a goodness that actively does things for others. Humility (tapeinophrosyne) is the same word Paul used negatively in Colossians 2:18 to describe false humility; here it is the genuine thing. Gentleness (prautes) is controlled strength, power under restraint. And patience (makrothymia), literally "long-tempered," is the capacity to absorb difficulty without retaliating. The phrase "the elect of God" connects the Colossian believers to Israel's own calling. Just as God chose Israel to be his special people and reflect his character to the world, believers in Jesus inherit that same identity and responsibility. Paul's references to the elect carry this same weight across his letters (Romans 8:33; 2 Timothy 2:10). Being chosen is not about favoritism; it is about mission.
Matthew 22:14

Compare also Matthew 24:22, 24, 31, where Jesus uses the same language in an eschatological context.

Peter reinforces Paul's point: being chosen carries a purpose. That purpose is to "proclaim the praises" of God, to live in a way that displays his character to the watching world. The privilege of election is inseparable from the responsibility of representation. You do not just belong to God; you display him.
Colossians 3:13
☩ Open in Bible App
"bearing with one another and forgiving one another if anyone has a grievance against another. Just as the Lord has forgiven you, so you are also to forgive."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The Greek word for "bearing with" is anechomenoi, which literally means to hold up under, to endure. It describes the patience required for daily community life: tolerating habits that annoy you, personalities that grate on you, weaknesses you wish would just go away. Then Paul escalates: beyond tolerating, you must forgive. The word for forgive here is charizomenoi, from the same root as charis (grace). To forgive is literally to "grace" someone, to extend unmerited favor. And the standard is devastating in its simplicity: "Just as the Lord has forgiven you." Not as much as you feel comfortable giving. Not proportional to how sorry they are. As Christ forgave you: completely, first, at great cost, without waiting for you to deserve it. Compare Matthew 5:7 and Matthew 6:14, where Jesus connects our experience of mercy directly to our willingness to extend it.
Colossians 3:14
☩ Open in Bible App
"But above all these things put on love, which is the bond of perfection."
New King James Version · NKJV
"Above all, put on love, which is the perfect bond of unity."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The Greek phrase syndesmos tes teleiotetos describes love as the binding agent, the fastener that holds all the other virtues together. Without love, compassion becomes condescending. Kindness becomes transactional. Humility becomes false modesty. Forgiveness becomes grudging tolerance. Love is not one more item on the list; it is the belt that cinches the entire garment. It is what makes the other seven qualities a unity rather than a checklist. And as 1 John 4:11-12 makes clear, the source of this love is God himself: "If God loved us in this way, we also must love one another."

John identifies the mechanism: God's love becomes visible in the world through how believers treat one another. No one has ever seen God directly, but when Christians love each other with the same self-giving love Christ demonstrated, God becomes visible through them. The invisible God becomes tangible through community.
🎯 Street Level
The Uniform You Put On Every Morning

Think about what happens when someone puts on a uniform for work. A nurse, a firefighter, a barista. The moment the uniform goes on, expectations shift. You are no longer just yourself; you are representing something larger. People look at you and see the institution, not just the individual. If you are rude in that uniform, the whole organization takes the hit. If you are kind, generous, patient, the organization gets the credit.

That is exactly what Paul is describing. Believers have "put on the new self" (verse 10). Now Paul says: here is what the uniform of the new self actually looks like. Compassion. Kindness. Humility. Gentleness. Patience. Forgiveness. Love. These are not personality traits you either have or you do not. They are the uniform of someone who represents Christ. You put them on deliberately, every single morning, whether you feel like it or not. And the watching world will judge your King by how you wear them.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
Harvard Researchers Confirm What Paul Already Knew

In January 2025, researchers at Harvard's T.H. Chan School of Public Health published findings linking habitual kindness, including volunteering and compassion toward others, to measurable improvements in physical health and longevity. Professor Laura Kubzansky noted that people who make a regular habit of helping others experience lower levels of physical pain, reduced mortality risk, and better physical functioning into old age. A separate Harvard study found that acts of kindness reduce cortisol (the body's primary stress hormone) and can even slow the biological aging process by protecting telomeres, the DNA structures that determine how long cells live.

A 2025 meta-analysis published in Scientific Reports examined 54 effect sizes across multiple studies and found a consistent, statistically significant link between compassion for others and personal well-being, regardless of age, gender, or culture. The benefits held across psychological well-being, social connection, and positive affect.

Paul was not thinking about cortisol levels when he wrote Colossians 3:12. But when he told the church that showing love, mercy, kindness, and forgiveness blesses both the giver and the receiver, he was describing a reality that modern science is only now catching up to. The qualities Paul prescribes are not burdens placed on believers; they are the design specifications for how humans were built to function. When you live in compassion and forgiveness, your body knows it. When you live in bitterness and resentment, your body knows that too.

✝️ The Christ Connection

Every quality Paul lists in Colossians 3:12-14 is a quality Jesus demonstrated first. Compassion: he wept at Lazarus's tomb and was moved to the depths of his being by human suffering. Kindness: he touched lepers, ate with tax collectors, and blessed children others pushed aside. Humility: he washed the feet of the men who would abandon him within hours. Gentleness: he did not break a bruised reed or snuff out a smoldering wick. Patience: he endured three years of misunderstanding from the very disciples he was training. Forgiveness: from the cross, he prayed, "Father, forgive them, for they don't know what they are doing."

And love. Above all, love. The love that held him on the cross when he had every right and every power to come down. The love that paid a debt he did not owe because we owed a debt we could not pay. Paul does not say "put on virtues." He says put on the character of the one who gave everything for you. These are not abstract ideals. They are the lived biography of Jesus Christ. To "put on" compassion, kindness, and forgiveness is to put on Christ himself.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Think of one person you are struggling to extend grace to right now. Before today ends, do one specific, concrete act of kindness toward them: send a text checking in on them, pray for them by name, or simply speak well of them to someone else. Not because they deserve it. Because Christ did it for you first.
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday's study walked through the "take off" side of conversion: putting to death the old self's habits of sexual immorality, greed, anger, malice, slander, and lying. Today completes the picture by showing what goes on in their place. Paul's argument is not simply "stop doing bad things." It is a full wardrobe change. The old garments come off, the new garments go on, and the new garments are specifically the character traits of Christ himself. Removal without replacement leaves a vacuum. The new life is not defined by what you stopped doing; it is defined by what you started becoming.

🙏 Closing Prayer

Father, thank you for not leaving us empty after stripping away the old self. You have given us something beautiful to put on: the very character of your Son. Today, help us wear compassion when impatience would be easier. Help us choose kindness when indifference feels safer. Help us forgive the way we have been forgiven, not because anyone has earned it, but because Jesus paid for it at the cross. Where we have been holding onto grudges, loosen our grip. Where we have been withholding grace, open our hands. Let the love that held Christ on the cross hold us together as your people today. In Jesus' name, Amen.
