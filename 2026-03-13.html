📌 Week in Review

This week Paul walked us through the complete blueprint for the new life in Christ: what to aim for, what to kill, what to strip off, what to put on, and how to sustain it all day by day.

  • ☩Sabbath: Living With Christ. Paul's thesis: if you have been raised with Christ, everything about how you live should reflect that reality. The memory text (Colossians 3:14, NKJV) set the capstone: love is the bond of perfection.
  • ☩Sunday: Heavenly-Mindedness. Seek what is above. Set your mind on things above. Your life is hidden with Christ in God, and when he appears, you will appear with him in glory.
  • ☩Monday: End Earthliness. Put to death what belongs to your earthly nature: sexual immorality, impurity, lust, evil desire, and greed. These are not minor habits; they are idolatry, and God's wrath is coming because of them.
  • ☩Tuesday: Renewed in Knowledge. Strip off the old self and put on the new, being renewed according to the image of your Creator. In Christ, every ethnic, cultural, and social boundary dissolves.
  • ☩Wednesday: Character of the New Life. Put on compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness, patience, forgiveness, and above all, love, the perfect bond of unity.
  • ☩Thursday: Living the New Life. Let the peace of Christ rule your hearts, let the word of Christ dwell richly among you through psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs, and do everything in the name of the Lord Jesus.
🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
Why Colossians 3 Was Not Obvious

To a modern reader, Colossians 3 can sound like standard Christian ethics: be kind, forgive, love each other, sing hymns. But to the first-century Colossians, almost nothing in this chapter was obvious. They lived in a world where identity was fixed at birth by ethnicity, social class, and citizenship. The idea that a Jewish convert, a Greek merchant, a Scythian laborer, and a Roman slave could share one identity, one wardrobe of virtues, and one internal umpire was genuinely radical. There was no cultural precedent for it.

The false teachers in Colossae had offered their own version of transformation: ascetic discipline, angel worship, mystical visions, and rigid rule-keeping. Paul's alternative is shockingly different. He grounds character change not in willpower or esoteric knowledge but in union with a Person. You died with Christ, so kill the old. You were raised with Christ, so live the new. You are chosen by God, so wear his character. The entire ethical program of Colossians 3 hangs on a single hinge: identity in Christ precedes behavior change. The Colossians did not need another program. They needed to understand who they already were.

📖 Discussion & Reflection
1. What has been your own experience with the promise that you have been "justified by faith"? How has that promise changed your life? How is it connected to the idea that you have also "been raised with Christ"?
Romans 5:1
☩ Open in Bible App
"Therefore, since we have been justified by faith, we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul's letter to Rome establishes justification by faith as the foundation of everything. This is the verse the converted soul "breaks forth into a new song" over: the transgression has been forgiven, the sin covered, peace with God secured. But Colossians 3 reveals that justification is not the finish line; it is the starting blocks. Having been justified (Romans 5:1), we have been raised with Christ (Colossians 3:1). Justification declares your status; resurrection with Christ activates your new life. One is legal; the other is experiential. Both are real, and neither works without the other.

The connection is vital: justification gives you peace with God (your standing changes), and being raised with Christ gives you a new orientation (your direction changes). A person who is justified but never lives as one raised with Christ has a passport to a country they never visit. A person who tries to live the raised life without resting in justification will burn out trying to earn what was already given.

2. What does it mean to be heavenly-minded? Is it more important than doing earthly good? Where is the balance?

Paul answers this question by his own structure. He opens Colossians 3 in heaven ("seek the things above") and immediately lands on earth ("put to death what belongs to your earthly nature"). The heavenly-minded person, in Paul's vision, is not someone gazing at the sky with folded hands. It is someone whose awareness of their hidden life in Christ produces the most practical, ground-level ethical living imaginable: forgiveness, kindness, patience, love, peace, gratitude. Heavenly-mindedness is the root system. Earthly good is the visible fruit.

The famous saying "so heavenly-minded they're no earthly good" actually reverses Paul's logic. In Colossians 3, the people most likely to do earthly good are precisely those who have set their minds on things above, because their identity is secure, their sins are dealt with, and their character is being shaped by a Person rather than a program. The balance is not 50/50; it is root and fruit. Get the root right and the fruit follows.

3. Think about the kind of influence your life has on others. And though we tend to think of this in the context of individual influence, what about our influence as a church? How does your local church impact your local community?

This question lands differently in 2025 than it would have a decade ago. According to data from Pushpay's network of over 14,000 churches, volunteering within church communities surged 19% year-over-year in 2025, and group participation jumped 22%. A separate report from the Do Good Institute found that the single strongest predictor of both volunteering and charitable giving in America is belonging to a religious congregation. People who are active in a faith community are 14.3 percentage points more likely to volunteer and 8.6 percentage points more likely to donate to causes beyond their own church.

But Paul's vision in Colossians 3 goes deeper than volunteer hours. The "whatever you do" filter of verse 17 applies to how the church treats the single mother who walks in off the street, how it responds to the homeless man at the door, whether its internal relationships display the compassion, kindness, and forgiveness Paul prescribes or devolve into the anger, slander, and malice he forbids. The most powerful community influence a church has is not its programs; it is whether people encounter the character of Christ when they encounter its members.

4. Read Colossians 3:11. What should this tell us about the unity we should have in Christ?
Colossians 3:11
☩ Open in Bible App
"In Christ there is not Greek and Jew, circumcision and uncircumcision, barbarian, Scythian, slave and free; but Christ is all and in all."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul's list is deliberately comprehensive. "Greek and Jew" covers the fundamental ethnic divide of his world. "Circumcision and uncircumcision" covers the religious boundary. "Barbarian, Scythian" covers the cultural spectrum from the foreign to the despised (Scythians were considered the most uncivilized people in the ancient world). "Slave and free" covers the social hierarchy. Paul is systematically dismantling every category the ancient world used to sort people into groups that matter and groups that do not. In Christ, every one of those categories is overridden by a single, all-encompassing identity: Christ is all and in all.

The challenge for the modern church is that we have created our own versions of these categories. Political affiliation, educational background, worship style preference, economic status, race, nationality. Paul does not say these differences disappear; he says they are no longer the basis for how we see or treat each other. The unity Paul describes is not uniformity. It is a community where the deepest thing about every person in the room is the same: Christ lives in them. When that identity is primary, the other categories lose their power to divide.

🌍 Still Speaking
The Daily Battle Paul Warned About Is Still Daily

One of the most honest observations about the Christian life is that conversion is not the end of the fight; it is the beginning. The justified believer does not fold their hands. Old habits, inherited tendencies toward wrong, and the constant pressure of an unregenerate culture will strive for mastery every single day. Each day requires a renewed consecration. Each day involves a genuine battle with evil.

This is not pessimism. It is the most freeing thing a new believer can hear, because it normalizes the struggle. If you expected conversion to eliminate temptation and you still face it daily, the temptation itself can feel like evidence that your faith is failing. Paul's entire argument in Colossians 3 says the opposite: the fight is proof you are alive. Dead people do not struggle. Only the raised struggle, because they have something worth fighting for and a power source that never runs dry. The promise is not that the battle ends but that the supply of Christ's strength for that battle is inexhaustible. At every step, you touch his living power.

✝️ Christ at the Center of the Week

Every day this week pointed to the same Person. Sunday: your life is hidden with Christ in God. Monday: you have died with Christ, so put to death what belongs to the old life. Tuesday: the new self is being renewed according to the image of its Creator. Wednesday: forgive as the Lord has forgiven you, and put on love. Thursday: let the peace of Christ rule, let the word of Christ dwell, and do everything in the name of the Lord Jesus.

The new life is not a self-improvement project with a spiritual veneer. It is Christ living in you, Christ shaping you, Christ sustaining you, Christ fighting through you, and Christ waiting at the end to appear with you in glory. The week began with your life hidden in him. It ends with the recognition that at every step you touch his living power. He is not the background to the new life. He is the new life.

✅ Weekend Practice
Before Sabbath begins, read Colossians 3:1-17 in one sitting. As you read, mentally walk through the week: the heavenly mindset, the things to kill, the things to strip off, the things to put on, the peace that rules, the word that dwells, the name that covers everything. Then choose one quality from Paul's "put on" list that you struggled with most this week. Carry it into the Sabbath as your one focus. Ask Christ to dress you in it by his power, not yours.

You told us to seek what is above, to put to death what belongs below, to strip off the old self and put on the character of your Son. Dress us again in Christ. And let the watching world see something real. In Jesus' name, Amen.
