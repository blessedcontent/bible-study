📌 TL;DR
Rules that look like wisdom can be the most effective substitute for the real thing, because they feel spiritual while requiring nothing of the heart.
  • ☩Colossians 2:20–23 is Paul's final indictment of the Colossian heresy: if you died with Christ, you died to the basic principles of the world. So why are you living as if you still belong to those principles, submitting to a list of "don't touch, don't taste, don't handle"?
  • ☩Keeping a fulfilled ceremonial law doesn't deepen faith; it turns a God-given gift into a human burden. When religious observances outlive their divine mandate, they become commandments of men, and the Pharisees and scribes demonstrated exactly what that looks like at full scale.
  • ☩Religious rule-keeping has a built-in danger: it breeds a sense of superiority over those who don't keep the same rules, and quietly slides from obedience to merit-seeking, the belief that spiritual performance earns standing before God.

🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
When the Earthly Temple Met Its End

When Jesus died on the Cross, something dramatic happened inside the Jerusalem temple: the massive curtain separating the Holy Place from the Most Holy Place tore from top to bottom (Matthew 27:51). This was not incidental. That veil had been the physical boundary between humanity and the presence of God, no one could enter except the high priest, once a year, on the Day of Atonement. When Christ died as the final atonement, the barrier was removed by divine hand, from top to bottom, meaning God tore it, not human hands. The access Christ provided made the entire earthly temple system redundant.

Paul describes the earthly temple and its ceremonies as belonging to "the basic principles of the world": things of this earth, this age, this passing order. Our citizenship, he argues, is in heaven (Philippians 3:20). The Colossian Jewish-Christian believers were clinging to a system whose divine mandate had been fulfilled and whose physical center would be destroyed entirely in AD 70. Paul's question in verse 20 carries a note of incredulity: you have died with Christ to these elemental things. Why are you living as though you still belong to the world that is passing away?

📖 Into the Word

Today's anchor passage is Colossians 2:20–23, the conclusion of Paul's sustained argument through all of Colossians 2. Having established that Christ is the fullness of God (2:9), that the sin-debt was cancelled at the Cross (2:14), and that the ceremonial shadows have met their substance (2:17), Paul now delivers his verdict on the whole religious program being imposed on the Colossians: it has the appearance of wisdom, but it is of no value. To understand why, the passage draws on the parallel language in Galatians 4, Jesus's confrontation with the Pharisees in Mark 7, and the torn temple veil in Matthew 27, all pointing to the same conclusion that human religious additions to what Christ has finished are not depth; they are drift.

Colossians 2:20–22
☩ Open in Bible App
"If you died with Christ to the basic principles of the world, why, as though you still belonged to the world, do you submit to its regulations: 'Don't handle, don't taste, don't touch'? All these regulations refer to what is destined to perish by being used up; they are human commands and doctrines."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The Greek phrase stoicheia tou kosmou, "basic principles of the world", appears here for the second time in Colossians (also in verse 8). Paul uses the same phrase in Galatians 4:3 and 4:9 to describe the elementary, pre-Christian religious order that Christ came to free us from. Stoicheia literally means things arranged in a row, like letters of an alphabet. Paul's point is that these regulations belong to the ABCs of a religious world that Christ has now moved beyond. The "don't handle, don't taste, don't touch" list is a caricature of the ascetic prohibitions being pushed in Colossae, rules about ritual purity, food, and physical contact that had accumulated far beyond the original Mosaic commands. Paul's verdict is blunt: these things are "destined to perish by being used up." They are temporal, physical, passing. They belong to the old order that is fading away.
Colossians 2:8 & 20 (NKJV)
☩ Open in Bible App
"Beware lest anyone cheat you through philosophy and empty deceit, according to the tradition of men, according to the basic principles of the world, and not according to Christ... Therefore, if you died with Christ from the basic principles of the world, why, as though living in the world, do you subject yourselves to regulations..."
New King James Version · NKJV
Paul frames the entire Colossian problem around this phrase. In verse 8 he warns against being taken captive by teaching that follows "the basic principles of the world, and not according to Christ." In verse 20 he reminds them they have died to those same principles. The contrast is stark: worldly religious systems on one side, Christ on the other. These are not complementary. A system organized around rules and regulations belongs to the passing world order; a life organized around Christ belongs to the new creation. The question is not whether the rules seem wise or spiritual. The question is: according to what source? Human tradition or Christ?
Galatians 4:3, 9
☩ Open in Bible App
"In the same way we also, when we were children, were in slavery under the basic elements of the world... But now, since you know God, or rather have become known by God, how can you turn back again to the weak and worthless basic elements? Do you want to be enslaved to them all over again?"
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul's parallel argument in Galatians uses identical language, and the emotional tone is even sharper: he calls the stoicheia "weak and worthless": not because they were always wrong, but because they have been superseded. A child's alphabet primer is valuable when you are learning to read. It becomes worthless the moment you can read, not because it was bad, but because it was introductory. Paul's Galatian argument adds a devastating rhetorical question: you have grown up into full knowledge of God through Christ. Why would you go back to the primers? Going back is not humility; it is a refusal of what Christ has given.
Matthew 27:51
☩ Open in Bible App
"Suddenly, the curtain of the sanctuary was torn in two from top to bottom, the earth quaked, and the rocks were split."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The torn veil is the physical, historical sign that the ceremonial regulations had been fulfilled and their divine mandate completed. The curtain was not worn out or damaged, it was torn, violently, from top to bottom, meaning the tearing originated above. God ended the system from his side. The elaborate regulations that governed who could enter, how, when, and under what conditions of ritual purity. All of it was made obsolete in the moment Christ died. For Jewish believers to insist on those regulations afterward was not faithfulness to God's commands; it was failure to recognize that God himself had declared the system complete. Compare Daniel 9:27, which Paul cites as a parallel: the prophecy of the one who would "put an end to sacrifice and offering", fulfilled at the Cross.
Mark 7:1–13
☩ Open in Bible App
"The Pharisees and some of the scribes who had come from Jerusalem gathered around him. They observed that some of his disciples were eating bread with unclean, that is, unwashed, hands... He said to them, 'Isaiah was right when he prophesied about you hypocrites, as it is written: This people honors me with their lips, but their heart is far from me. They worship me in vain, teaching as doctrines human commands. Abandoning the command of God, you hold on to human tradition... You have a fine way of invalidating God's command in order to set up your tradition!'"
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Jesus's confrontation with the Pharisees in Mark 7 is the definitive illustration of what human commandments layered on top of divine law actually produce. The Pharisees had taken the Mosaic purity laws and built an elaborate fence of oral tradition around them, hundreds of additional rules designed to ensure the original commands were never broken. The result was a system so complex and so focused on external compliance that it had completely displaced the heart of what God was after. Jesus quotes Isaiah's exact diagnosis: "their heart is far from me." Religious rule-making, at sufficient density, produces exactly the opposite of its stated goal. It creates the appearance of devotion while hollowing out the reality. The Colossian situation is the same dynamic in a different context.
Colossians 2:23
☩ Open in Bible App
"Although these have a reputation for wisdom by promoting self-made religion, false humility, and severe treatment of the body, they are not of any value in curbing self-indulgence."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
This is Paul's most devastating line in the entire section. The ascetic program has "a reputation for wisdom", it looks spiritually serious. The self-denial, the dietary restrictions, the elaborate ritual humility: from the outside, it reads as deep commitment. But Paul cuts straight to the diagnostic: it "is not of any value in curbing self-indulgence." The flesh is not defeated by starving the body. The appetites of the sinful nature are not addressed by external regulation. What the ascetic system actually produces, at its worst, is pride: the most intractable appetite of all. A person can abstain from every food on the prohibited list while their inner life is consumed by self-congratulation and contempt for those who don't. Rules without regeneration do not produce holiness. They produce performance.
2 Peter 3:13
☩ Open in Bible App
"But based on his promise, we wait for new heavens and a new earth, where righteousness dwells."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul's argument that the ceremonial system belongs to the "basic principles of the world" is grounded in an eschatological conviction: this world is passing away, and what we are waiting for is something qualitatively new. The regulations that belong to the earthly order: food laws tied to physical matter, purity rules governing what you touch: belong to a world that is temporary. Clinging to those regulations means orienting your life around what is fading rather than what is coming. Peter's vision of new heavens and a new earth where righteousness dwells is the destination. Everything that belongs to the passing order should be held loosely, not as the center of one's spiritual identity.
🎯 Street Level
The App That Never Updates

Imagine an app that was genuinely groundbreaking when it launched: it solved a real problem, it was beautifully designed for its time, people built their workflows around it. Then the underlying technology it was built on was completely replaced by something better. The original developer pushed a final update: "all features now native in the new system." But a community of loyal users kept running the old app anyway: not because it still worked better, but because they had invested so much in it, because it felt familiar, and because some of them had built their identity around being expert users of it.

Paul's point about the Colossian ceremonial system is exactly this. The system was real, it was from God, it served a genuine purpose. Then Christ arrived and every feature it pointed to became natively available in him. The Cross was the final update: "fulfilled, complete, no longer required." Continuing to run the old system after that is not faithfulness: it is a refusal to let what God finished actually be finished. And the people most invested in the old system are often the most resistant to recognizing that the new one has arrived.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
When Religious Rules Become the Gospel

In 2024 and 2025, researchers studying Christian nationalism and legalistic church cultures documented a consistent pattern: congregations that organize their identity primarily around behavioral codes: specific dress standards, dietary rules, entertainment prohibitions, and Sabbath-keeping practices, tend to develop what sociologists call "boundary maintenance culture." Members' spiritual standing is assessed less by their relationship with Christ and more by visible compliance with the community's rule set. Researchers at several university religion departments noted that exit interviews with people who had left such communities frequently cited not loss of faith, but exhaustion from a performance treadmill that never offered any sense of arrival.

Paul named this dynamic in verse 23 nearly two thousand years ago. A reputation for wisdom. Self-made religion. Severe treatment of the body. And ultimately: of no value in curbing the actual problem. The flesh is not defeated by rule lists. It is addressed by regeneration, by genuine connection to Christ as the head, by the inward work of the Spirit that produces fruit no external regulation can manufacture. The question the passage presses on every religious community, including Adventist ones: are our practices pointing people toward Christ, or have they quietly become the point themselves?

✝️ The Christ Connection

The guide's closing line for today cuts to the heart of everything: Christ himself is the fountain from which springs the truth of the Scriptures. Not the interpretive tradition. Not the religious authority. Not the accumulated wisdom of Bible experts. Christ. He is the Word become flesh, the one to whom all Scripture points, and the one through whom all Scripture is rightly understood. Any religious system that places human interpretation between the believer and Christ as the source of truth has already started doing what the Pharisees did: invalidating the command of God to set up human tradition in its place.

And the foundation is not performance. Paul's discussion question for today states it as clearly as it can be stated: our only foundation for salvation is what Jesus has done for us, outside of us, in place of us, regardless of whatever he does in us. The work done in us by the Spirit is real and important. But it is never the foundation. The foundation is the finished work of Christ at the Cross: outside of us, completed before we had anything to contribute, and available to us through faith alone. Every human religious system that adds requirements to that foundation, however sincere, however ancient, however elaborately defended, has misunderstood what the Cross accomplished.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Paul's question is personal: are there religious practices in your life that have quietly shifted from pointing you to Christ to measuring your standing before God or others? Pick one specific practice today and ask honestly: is this helping me know Christ better, or is it functioning as a performance for my own sense of spiritual adequacy? You don't need to abandon it. You just need to be honest about what it is doing in your heart.
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday's study showed that the ceremonial calendar: festivals, new moons, sabbaths, were shadows pointing forward to Christ, and that once the substance arrived, the shadows had fulfilled their purpose. Today Paul draws the logical conclusion: if you continue submitting to those shadows as divine requirements after they have been fulfilled, you are not deepening your faith. You are submitting to human commands that have outlived their divine mandate. Yesterday was about recognizing that the shadows gave way to the substance. Today is about understanding what happens when you refuse to let them give way: they become, in Paul's precise language, "commandments of men."

Taken together, this week's studies through Colossians 2 form a single coherent warning: at every level: ceremonial calendar, ascetic practice, angel veneration, human interpretive authority: the Colossian error is the same. Something good was placed in the position that belongs only to Christ. Paul's remedy is not cynicism about religious practice. It is clarity about the source: Christ is the head. Everything else is a branch.

🙏 Closing Prayer

Lord, it is so easy to let practices become performance, to keep the form of devotion while the heart drifts somewhere else entirely. Today I want to come back to the simple truth that my only standing before you is what Jesus did, outside of me and in place of me. Not what I have done. Not how consistently I have kept the rules. Not how well I have performed. Just him, and what he finished. Help me hold my practices loosely enough that they can do what they were always meant to do: point me toward you, not replace you. In Jesus' name, Amen.
