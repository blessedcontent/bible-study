Moses, Humble Servant

Growing in a Relationship with God Lesson 3, Pride Versus Humility Part 4 of 7
April 14, 2026
✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR

The man who had every reason to be proud became the most humble person on the face of the earth, and it took 40 years in the wilderness to get him there.

  • ☩ Moses walked away from the most powerful education and position in the ancient world because he saw something Egypt could not offer
  • ☩ His impulsive act of violence cost him his self-confidence, his status, and 40 years of his life, but God used every bit of that loss
  • ☩ Scripture calls Moses the most humble man on earth, a title earned through brokenness, not talent
  • ☩ We cannot manufacture humility on our own; it is the product of seeing God clearly and ourselves honestly
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday we stood in the temple with two men: a Pharisee who performed his righteousness for God and a tax collector who simply confessed his need. We learned that self-knowledge begins not by comparing ourselves to others but by standing before God's holiness. Today we meet someone who put that principle into practice across an entire lifetime. Moses had every reason to be proud, every qualification the ancient world valued, and every excuse to stay comfortable. Instead, he chose a different path, was broken on it, and emerged as the most humble human being Scripture ever named.

🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
The Education That Ruled the World

To understand what Moses gave up, you have to understand what Egypt offered. In the era scholars place Moses' upbringing, Egypt was the undisputed superpower of the ancient Near East. The royal court did not simply educate its princes; it engineered them. A prince raised in Pharaoh's household would have been trained in scribal arts, diplomatic protocol, military strategy, administrative law, engineering, mathematics, and the religious texts of a civilization that had been refining its knowledge for over a thousand years. Stephen's summary in Acts 7:22 is not exaggeration: Moses was educated in all the wisdom of the Egyptians and was powerful in both speech and action.

Walking away from that was not a career change. It was a renunciation of the most complete preparation for power the ancient world could produce. As a historian, poet, philosopher, general, and legislator, Moses stood without a peer. Yet he had the moral strength to refuse the flattering prospects of wealth, greatness, and fame, choosing instead to suffer with an enslaved nation that had nothing to offer him except a God who had made them a promise. That choice is the foundation everything else in his story is built on.

📖 Into the Word

Hebrews 11 is the Bible's hall of faith, and Moses occupies one of its longest entries. The writer of Hebrews does not focus on Moses' miracles or his leadership; instead, the spotlight falls on a series of choices that only make sense if Moses could see something invisible. What drove a man to walk away from everything? And how did that man, after a devastating failure, become the most humble person on the face of the earth? The answer is faith: a calculated decision to value what God promised over what Egypt displayed.

Hebrews 11:24-26
☩ Open in Bible App
"By faith Moses, when he had grown up, refused to be called the son of Pharaoh's daughter and chose to suffer with the people of God rather than to enjoy the fleeting pleasure of sin. For he considered reproach for the sake of Christ to be greater wealth than the treasures of Egypt, since he was looking ahead to the reward."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Three verbs define Moses' faith: he refused, he chose, and he considered. Each one is an act of will driven by sight that goes beyond the visible. The Greek pistei ("by faith") opens the sentence and governs everything that follows. Moses' decision was not emotional; it was evaluative. He weighed the treasures of Egypt against the oneidismos ("reproach, disgrace") of identifying with God's people, and he concluded that the disgrace was worth more. The word translated "fleeting" (proskairon) means "lasting for a season, temporary." Moses did the math that pride never does: he measured the lifespan of what Egypt offered against the lifespan of what God promised. The phrase "reproach for the sake of Christ" is remarkable. Moses lived centuries before the incarnation, yet Hebrews says he suffered for Christ. The writer is making a theological claim: every act of faith in the Old Testament, every choice to suffer for God's people rather than prosper with the world, participates in the same story that would culminate in Jesus. Moses was not just choosing between Egypt and Israel. He was choosing between the passing world and the eternal kingdom, the same choice 1 John 2:17 set before us yesterday. But Moses did not walk this path perfectly. After his initial stand with his people, he killed an Egyptian in an impulsive act of violence (Exodus 2:11-12). That act cost him everything: his position, his self-confidence, his plans, and 40 years of his life. He fled to Midian, where mountains replaced marble halls and sheep replaced soldiers. His self-sufficiency was stripped away. And it was precisely in that wilderness, with his pride ground to dust, that God appeared in a burning bush and called him to the greatest leadership task in the Old Testament. The palace education made Moses capable. The wilderness made Moses usable.
Numbers 12:3
☩ Open in Bible App
"(Now the man Moses was very humble, more than all men who were on the face of the earth.)"
New King James Version · NKJV
"Moses was a very humble man, more so than anyone on the face of the earth."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
This is one of the most extraordinary descriptions in all of Scripture. The Hebrew word is anav, meaning humble, lowly, meek. It does not mean passive or weak; it describes someone who has power but does not use it for self-promotion. Consider the context: this verse appears in the middle of a chapter where Aaron and Miriam are challenging Moses' authority. Instead of defending himself, Moses says nothing. God Himself steps in to defend him. That is the posture of anav: so secure in God's estimation that you do not need to fight for your own honor. Think about what humility looks like when you hold it against the list of events in Moses' life. The burning bush: Moses removed his sandals instead of demanding credentials. The plagues in Egypt: Moses was the instrument, not the hero. Crossing the Red Sea: Moses lifted his staff while God moved the water. Manna from heaven: Moses distributed what he did not create. Receiving the Ten Commandments: Moses carried what God wrote. In every single one of these moments, pride would have ruined the mission. If Moses had approached the burning bush the way the Pharisee approached the temple, the exodus would never have happened. Humility was not an accessory to Moses' leadership. It was the precondition for it.
🎯 Street Level
The Starter That Looks Like Failure

If you have ever made sourdough bread from scratch, you know that the process begins with something that looks like a mistake. You mix flour and water in a jar, set it on the counter, and wait. For the first two days, nothing happens. On day three, it starts to bubble, but it also starts to smell. By day five, the mixture looks like a small science experiment gone wrong: sticky, sour, unpromising. If you did not know what was happening, you would throw it out. It looks like decay. It smells like neglect. Every instinct says this has failed.

But it has not failed. What is happening inside that jar is fermentation: wild yeast is colonizing the mixture, lactic acid bacteria are building the flavor profile, and a living culture is forming that will have the power to make bread rise for years. The ugly, smelly, apparently useless stage is not a detour. It is the process. Without those days of sitting on the counter looking like nothing, the starter never develops the strength to do what it was made to do. You cannot rush it. You cannot skip the awkward phase. You can only feed it, wait, and trust that something is happening beneath the surface.

Moses spent 40 years in his fermentation stage. The palace gave him raw ingredients: knowledge, skill, confidence. But those ingredients were not yet alive. They needed to be broken down, reorganized, and colonized by something that only time and failure could produce. The wilderness was not punishment for killing the Egyptian. It was the counter where God set Moses down and let the slow, invisible work of humility transform raw capability into usable character. By the time the burning bush appeared, Moses had stopped smelling like the palace and started smelling like bread: the kind of person God could use to feed an entire nation. The seasons in your life that feel like fermentation, where nothing is happening and everything seems to be going wrong, may be the exact process that turns your raw ingredients into something that can actually rise.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
The Difficulty You Actually Need

Decades of cognitive science research, anchored by the work of Robert Bjork at UCLA and expanded by studies published through 2024 in journals like Educational Psychology Review, have documented a counterintuitive finding: learning that feels hard produces deeper, more durable understanding than learning that feels easy. Researchers call this principle "desirable difficulties." When students struggle with material, fail a practice test, or take the harder path through a problem, their brains form stronger memory traces and more flexible knowledge structures than students who glide through the same content without friction. The paradox is that struggle feels like failure, and ease feels like success, but the brain is doing the opposite of what the emotions report.

Moses' story is a 40-year case study in desirable difficulties. The Egyptian palace education gave him knowledge, confidence, and capability. It was the easy path, and it felt like success. The Midian wilderness gave him failure, silence, and sheep. It felt like the end. But it was the wilderness, not the palace, that produced the leader God needed. The palace made Moses impressive. The desert made him humble. And humility, it turns out, was the one qualification that mattered. The same principle operates in our spiritual lives. The seasons that feel like setbacks, the failures that strip away our self-sufficiency, the long stretches where nothing seems to be happening, may be the desirable difficulties God is using to build something in us that comfort never could.

✝️ The Christ Connection

Hebrews says Moses considered the "reproach for the sake of Christ" to be greater wealth than the treasures of Egypt. Moses could not have fully understood what that meant, but we can. Every choice Moses made, the refusal of Egyptian privilege, the willingness to suffer with God's people, the journey through a wilderness that broke his pride, pointed forward to a greater Moses who would make the same choices at an infinitely higher cost. Jesus refused the kingdoms of this world when Satan offered them (Matthew 4:8-10). He chose to suffer with sinners rather than remain in the glory He had shared with the Father from eternity. And He walked His own wilderness, not for 40 years but for 33, until the cross became the ultimate act of anav: infinite power choosing not to save itself, so that it could save everyone else.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Think about the "wilderness season" you are in right now, or the most recent one you went through. Instead of asking "Why is this happening to me?" ask "What is God building in me through this that comfort could not produce?" Write one specific thing that season has taught you about dependence on God rather than dependence on yourself.
🙏 Closing Prayer

Father, we confess that we prefer the palace to the wilderness. We want the education without the breaking, the calling without the cost, the leadership without the humility. But You know what we need better than we do. Thank You for the seasons that strip away our self-sufficiency, even when we do not understand them while we are living through them. Thank You for Moses, who shows us that the wilderness is not a dead end but a doorway. Make us patient in the process. Make us willing to be broken if breaking is what it takes to become usable. And when the burning bush finally appears, let us be humble enough to take off our shoes and listen. In Jesus' name, Amen.
