Thursday, Living the New Life

Colossians · Week 11, Day 6
March 12, 2026
✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR

Paul has told the Colossians what to take off and what to put on; now he tells them how the whole new life actually holds together: peace as the umpire, Scripture as the atmosphere, gratitude as the heartbeat, and everything done under one name.

  • ☩The peace of Christ is not a feeling but an internal authority; it "rules" (literally, umpires) in the heart, settling disputes and guiding decisions from the inside out.
  • ☩The word of Christ is meant to "dwell richly" in the community, not sit on a shelf. It takes up residence through teaching, admonishing, and singing together.
  • ☩Music is not decoration in Paul's vision of the church; it is a vehicle for theology, a means of teaching and encouraging one another through psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs.
  • ☩The capstone: whatever you do, in word or deed, do it all in the name of the Lord Jesus. This is the filter for every decision, every conversation, every action.
🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
Two Kinds of Peace in the Roman Empire

Every person in the Roman Empire knew the phrase pax Romana. It was the centerpiece of imperial propaganda: Rome had brought peace to the world. But everyone also knew what that peace actually looked like. It was maintained by legions, enforced by crucifixion, and purchased through submission. The pax Romana was peace imposed from the outside. Cross us, and the peace disappears.

Paul writes to believers living inside that empire and introduces a fundamentally different concept: the pax Christi, the peace of Christ. This peace does not come from Roman swords or political stability. It is not imposed from the outside at all. It "rules" from within, acting as an internal umpire that settles disputes, calms anxieties, and guides decisions. For people who lived under an empire that enforced conformity through violence, the idea of a peace that operates from the inside of the human heart, voluntarily and gratefully, was genuinely revolutionary.

📖 Into the Word

Today's anchor passage is Colossians 3:15-17, the closing movement of Paul's practical vision for the new community in Christ. After describing what to strip off (vv. 5-9), what to put on (vv. 12-14), and love as the binding agent (v. 14), Paul now describes the internal operating system of the new life: peace as the ruling principle, Scripture as the dwelling presence, music as the means of mutual instruction, and gratitude as the constant posture. The supporting passages (Ephesians 5:19, 1 Samuel 16:23, 1 Samuel 18:10-11) illustrate the power of music for both good and ill.

Colossians 3:15
☩ Open in Bible App
"And let the peace of Christ, to which you were also called in one body, rule your hearts. And be thankful."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The Greek word for "rule" here is brabeueto, and it carries a very specific image. A brabeutes was the umpire or referee at the Greek athletic games, the person who settled disputes, made final calls, and awarded prizes. Paul is saying: let the peace of Christ be the umpire in your heart. When two impulses compete, when anxiety and trust are both making their case, when resentment and forgiveness are both on the table, let Christ's peace make the call. This is not passive serenity. It is an active, decision-making authority operating inside you. And notice: this peace was the purpose of the calling. "You were called in one body" to this peace. It is not optional, and it is not individual. The peace of Christ is meant to function within community, settling disputes between believers the same way it settles conflicts within a single heart. The sentence ends with a two-word command that Paul clearly considers essential: "Be thankful." Gratitude is the natural response to peace, and the soil in which peace grows.
Colossians 3:16
☩ Open in Bible App
"Let the word of Christ dwell richly among you, in all wisdom teaching and admonishing one another through psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs, singing to God with gratitude in your hearts."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The verb enoikeito means to take up residence, to make a home, to settle in and stay. Paul is not saying "read your Bible occasionally." He is saying: let the word of Christ move in and furnish every room. The word plousios (richly) means abundantly, lavishly, without scarcity. Scripture should not be rationed in the life of a believer or a church; it should be the very atmosphere. The Greek text is somewhat ambiguous about the relationship between "teaching and admonishing" and "psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs," and scholars have long debated how the grammar connects. Music may itself be the vehicle of teaching and admonishing, not merely an accompaniment to it. Paul uses the same trio in Ephesians 5:19: "speaking to one another in psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs, singing and making music with your heart to the Lord." The three categories likely refer to the Old Testament Psalms (psalmois), early Christian compositions of praise (hymnois), and Spirit-prompted songs arising from the community's worship (odais pneumatikais). The words of the songs are themselves a means of teaching truth and instructing one another in how to live the new life.
Ephesians 5:19
☩ Open in Bible App
"speaking to one another in psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs, singing and making music with your heart to the Lord."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul's parallel passage in Ephesians adds an important detail: music in the early church was directed both horizontally ("to one another") and vertically ("to the Lord"). Worship music is not a performance for an audience; it is simultaneously a conversation with your brothers and sisters and an offering to God. When you sing in church, you are teaching and being taught at the same time.
Colossians 3:17
☩ Open in Bible App
"And whatever you do, in word or in deed, do everything in the name of the Lord Jesus, giving thanks to God the Father through him."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
This is one of the most sweeping statements in the New Testament. "Whatever you do" leaves no category untouched. "In word or in deed" covers every form of human activity. "In the name of the Lord Jesus" means under his authority, consistent with his character, in a way that represents him well. This is the filter Paul places over all of life: before you speak, before you act, can this be done in Jesus' name? If the answer is no, the action does not belong in the new life. And once again, Paul ends with thanksgiving. Gratitude is not a sidebar in Colossians 3; it is the thread running through every verse. Peace, Scripture, music, and action all operate in an atmosphere of thankfulness.
1 Samuel 16:23
☩ Open in Bible App
"Whenever the spirit from God troubled Saul, David would pick up his harp and play, and Saul would then be relieved, feel better, and the evil spirit would leave him."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The Old Testament provides its own powerful testimony to the influence of music on the human spirit. David's harp did not just entertain Saul; it brought genuine relief from spiritual torment. The Hebrew word for "relieved" (ravach) means to breathe freely, to find space, to experience release. Music created room in Saul's soul where the oppressive spirit could not stay. This foreshadows exactly what Paul is describing in Colossians 3: the right music, filled with truth and directed toward God, creates an internal environment where peace can rule and anxiety loses its grip.
1 Samuel 18:10-11
☩ Open in Bible App
"The next day an evil spirit sent from God came powerfully on Saul, and he began to rave inside the palace. David was playing the harp as usual, but Saul was holding a spear, and he threw it, thinking, 'I'll pin David to the wall.' But David got away from him twice."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The same musician, the same instrument, the same setting, but a completely different result. When Saul's heart was open, David's music brought healing. When Saul's heart was consumed with jealousy and resentment, the same music provoked violence. The lesson is sobering: music's power is real, but the condition of the heart determines whether that power brings relief or rage. This is why Paul specifies that the singing should be done "with gratitude in your hearts to God." The heart's posture matters as much as the song itself.
🎯 Street Level
The Playlist That Runs in the Background

Everyone has a mental playlist running in the background of their day. For some people it is a feed of anxious thoughts on repeat: what could go wrong, what already went wrong, what someone said that you cannot let go of. For others it is comparison, resentment, or self-criticism playing on shuffle all day long. You do not choose to play it. It just runs.

Paul is essentially saying: change the playlist. Let the word of Christ be the thing running in the background. Let Scripture, worship, and gratitude be the tracks that loop in your mind when nothing else is competing for your attention. Because whatever is playing in the background shapes what comes out of you when the pressure hits. If anxiety has been running all day, you will snap at the first provocation. If peace and thankfulness have been running, you will respond from a completely different place. Paul is not being metaphorical. He is describing the mechanics of spiritual formation: what you fill your mind with determines who you become.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
Science Catches Up to Paul on Music and Anxiety

In June 2025, researchers at the University of Melbourne published the largest meta-analysis ever conducted on music therapy and anxiety. Published in eClinicalMedicine (a Lancet journal), the study analyzed data from clinical trials spanning multiple decades and found that music therapy produced significant reductions in anxiety across mental health, medical, and work-related settings. Receptive music formats (listening) and combined approaches (listening plus active participation) were the most effective. The researchers noted that music therapy's anxiety-reducing effects were comparable to those of first-line psychotherapies and pharmacological treatments.

The study also found that music therapy reduced physiological markers of stress, including heart rate and blood pressure. A separate 2025 scoping review in JMIR Mental Health confirmed that music interventions, particularly those involving intentional listening, reduced cortisol levels and improved emotional regulation across diverse populations.

Paul told the Colossians to let the word of Christ dwell richly among them through "psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs." He was not prescribing background noise. He was describing a practice that modern neuroscience now confirms reshapes the brain's stress response, reduces anxiety at a physiological level, and creates the internal conditions where peace can actually rule. David's harp soothed Saul's tormented spirit three thousand years ago. The mechanism, it turns out, is not mysterious at all. Music changes the body. And when the music carries truth, it changes the soul.

✝️ The Christ Connection

Notice whose peace rules, whose word dwells, and whose name covers everything. It is the peace of Christ. The word of Christ. The name of the Lord Jesus. This is not a self-help program with spiritual vocabulary. Every element of the new life Paul describes is anchored in a Person. The peace that umpires your heart is the same peace Jesus spoke over the storm on Galilee and the same peace he breathed on his disciples after the resurrection: "Peace be with you" (John 20:19). The word that dwells richly is not abstract wisdom; it is the revelation of the one who is himself the Word made flesh (John 1:14). And doing everything "in the name of the Lord Jesus" is possible only because Jesus earned that name by humbling himself to the point of death on a cross, after which God exalted him and gave him the name above every name (Philippians 2:9).

The new life is not a behavior modification program. It is a life lived inside the reality of who Jesus is, what Jesus did, and what Jesus is doing in you right now.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Replace one thing in your daily routine today. If you normally start the morning with news or social media, start instead with a psalm or a worship song. If your commute defaults to talk radio or podcasts, switch to music that carries truth. Pay attention to how the change affects your internal state for the rest of the day. Paul says the word of Christ is meant to "dwell richly" in you; give it some actual real estate today.
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday's study gave us the "put on" list: compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness, patience, forgiveness, and above all, love as the binding agent. Today Paul answers the natural follow-up question: how does a community actually sustain those qualities day after day? His answer is a three-part operating system. First, the peace of Christ serves as the internal umpire, guiding decisions and settling conflicts. Second, the word of Christ provides the atmosphere, dwelling richly through teaching, admonishing, and singing. Third, everything is done under the authority of Jesus' name, with thanksgiving as the constant posture. Yesterday was the wardrobe. Today is the daily rhythm that keeps it on.

🙏 Closing Prayer

Father, thank you for not just telling us what to wear but showing us how to live. Let your peace be the umpire in our hearts today, settling every internal dispute with the authority of what Christ has already accomplished. Let your word take up residence in us, not as a guest passing through but as a permanent inhabitant that fills every room. And wherever we go, whatever we say, whatever we do, let it be done under the name of Jesus, the name you exalted above every other name. Change our playlists. Fill our minds with truth and our mouths with gratitude. And when the pressure comes, let what comes out of us be evidence that your word has been dwelling richly on the inside. In Jesus' name, Amen.
