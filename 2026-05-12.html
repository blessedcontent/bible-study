Jesus Teaches Us How to Pray

Growing in a Relationship with God Lesson 7, Practical Prayer Part 4 of 7
May 12, 2026
✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR

Twelve men who had been reciting Jewish prayer their whole lives watched their Rabbi pray once and asked Him a question they had never asked anyone: teach us how You do that.

  • ☩Jesus opens His teaching by warning against ostentatious public prayer and against the multiplied-words prayer of the Gentiles, then gives the disciples nine plain lines.
  • ☩The address "Our Father in heaven" is the prayer's most radical move: the Son who has been calling the Father Abba from before time hands the family word to the disciples.
  • ☩The first three petitions are God-ward (Your name, Your kingdom, Your will), and the next three are us-ward (bread, forgiveness, deliverance), in that exact order.
  • ☩The whole prayer is a template, not a script. Luke records a shorter version. Both come from the same Teacher; the rhythm is what matters, not the exact words.
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday Hannah's lips were moving at the doorpost of the tabernacle, and the priest mistook her silent prayer for drunkenness. Today the disciples are watching their Teacher do something they had never seen before. Jewish prayer life was not something they had to be introduced to. They had been reciting the Shema since they could speak, and the Eighteen Benedictions had been on their lips three times a day for as long as they could remember. They were not asking Jesus for words. They had plenty of words. They were asking Him for whatever it was He had that they had been missing. And the Greek verb behind their request, didasko, "teach," is the same verb a rabbi's students used to say "make us your apprentices in this." The day Hannah ran out of words was the day the verb shaphak got its silent test case. The day the disciples ran out of theirs is the day the verb finally hears its Master answer.

🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
Prayer in the Synagogue Court

By the first century, Jewish prayer life had a rhythm older than the temple. Three times a day a faithful Jew prayed: Shacharit at sunrise, Mincha in the afternoon, Maariv after dark. The centerpiece of each was the Amidah, "the Standing," eighteen benedictions covering everything from the patriarchs to the dispersion to the coming of Messiah. On Sabbaths and feasts there were extra liturgies. The Shema, recited morning and evening, anchored the day in the confession that the LORD is one. Children memorized large portions of the Psalter before they could read. The disciples had been doing this for thirty years. None of them needed Jesus to teach them a prayer.

What they needed was something else. Pharisaic teachers in their day had built a culture of public prayer that turned a worship act into a status display. Long, elaborate, carefully constructed prayers were spoken on the corner of major streets at the appointed hour, where there happened to be a maximum audience. The Greek world had its own version: pile up beautiful phrases, name as many divine epithets as you could fit, hope the gods notice the rhetoric. Both reflexes were alive in the courtyards Jesus walked through. The disciples watched Jesus slip away before sunrise to a quiet hillside, and they watched Him come back changed. They had been raised in a religious culture that prayed louder and longer than anyone in the ancient world. He showed them that prayer was a private fire in a quiet room, lit before the morning made any noise. The Lord's Prayer is short partly because it was given to a generation already drowning in long prayers, and the Teacher knew the difference between volume and intimacy.

📖 Into the Word

The disciples saw their Rabbi pray. Luke tells us they saw Him slip away "to deserted places" in Luke 5:16, spend a whole night in prayer before choosing the Twelve in Luke 6:12, and pray alone on the way to the cross in Luke 9:18 and Luke 22:41. Even the meal prayers in Luke 24:30 opened blind eyes. Mark adds the early-morning desolate place in Mark 1:35 and the after-supper mountain in Mark 6:46. The disciples were not asking out of ignorance; they were asking out of hunger. "Lord, teach us to pray" lands in Luke 11:1, and the Teacher does not refuse.

Matthew 6:5-15

"Whenever you pray, you must not be like the hypocrites, because they love to pray standing in the synagogues and on the street corners to be seen by people. Truly I tell you, they have their reward. But when you pray, go into your private room, shut your door, and pray to your Father who is in secret. And your Father who sees in secret will reward you. When you pray, don't babble like the Gentiles, since they imagine they'll be heard for their many words. Don't be like them, because your Father knows the things you need before you ask him. Therefore, you should pray like this: Our Father in heaven, your name be honored as holy. Your kingdom come. Your will be done on earth as it is in heaven. Give us today our daily bread. And forgive us our debts, as we also have forgiven our debtors. And do not bring us into temptation, but deliver us from the evil one. For if you forgive others their offenses, your heavenly Father will forgive you as well. But if you don't forgive others, your offenses will not be forgiven by your Father."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

Jesus opens with two warnings before He gives the prayer. Don't pray to be seen, and don't pile up words trying to be heard. The hypocrites had the audience problem; the Gentiles had the volume problem. Authentic prayer dies in either failure mode. The fix in Matthew 6:5-8 is a private room and a shut door, and the reason is that the Father "knows the things you need before you ask him." Prayer is not informing God of facts He missed. It is meeting the Father who already knows.

The address is the prayer's most radical line. Pater hemon, "Our Father." The Aramaic word Jesus probably used in everyday speech was Abba, the small intimate word a Jewish child used at the kitchen table. The Son who has been calling the Father by that name from before the foundation of the world hands creatures the same address. "Our" is not a mistake either. The disciples were being taught a corporate prayer for a corporate family, even when they prayed it alone in a closet. The first three petitions are God-ward. Hagiastheto to onoma sou, "your name be honored as holy"; eltheto he basileia sou, "your kingdom come"; genetheto to thelema sou, "your will be done." Three deliberate orientations of the heart before any request for ourselves shows up. By Friday we will see how Daniel's prayer used the same instinct, four centuries earlier, on a Babylonian afternoon.

Then the prayer turns toward us. The Greek word in "give us today our daily bread" is epiousion, an oddly shaped word that does not appear anywhere else in Greek literature outside the Lord's Prayer. The early church wrestled with whether it meant "for the day," "for tomorrow," or "for our existence." Jerome translated it "supersubstantial." The honest answer is that the Greek itself is bent toward bread that is just enough, and only enough, for the present and the next step. Then the prayer asks for what is harder than bread: forgiveness, in the same currency we have offered other people. Then deliverance from temptation, in the language of Psalm 91's shelter under the wing. The doxology that historically closes the prayer ("for thine is the kingdom and the power and the glory") echoes 1 Chronicles 29:11, David's prayer over the temple offering, where the king publicly handed back to the LORD everything he had been given.

Then verses 14 and 15 do something a little startling. Of all the petitions, Jesus circles back to the forgiveness one and underlines it twice. Forgive others, He says, or you will not be forgiven yourself. The hardest line of the whole prayer is also the line He repeats. Practical prayer, by His definition, is bound up with practical mercy. You cannot pour out a clenched fist.

Luke 11:2-4
☩ Open in Bible App
"He said to them, 'Whenever you pray, say, Father, your name be honored as holy. Your kingdom come. Give us each day our daily bread. And forgive us our sins, for we ourselves also forgive everyone in debt to us. And do not bring us into temptation.'"
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

Luke's version is shorter. The opening drops "Our" and "in heaven" and just says "Father." The third God-ward petition (Your will be done) is missing. The deliverance line is shorter. The closing doxology is not in Luke at all. The disciples almost certainly heard Jesus pray something close to this prayer many times over many months. The two Gospel writers are not contradicting each other; they are remembering two different occasions, or two slightly different deliveries of the same teaching. The point of comparing them is not to figure out which is "the original," but to see the freedom Jesus left us inside the rhythm.

"Father" alone is enough. The address is the prayer. Everything that follows is just opening doors inside a relationship that already exists. Sincerity, simplicity, daily, intimate, willing to forgive: this is the architecture. The reader who memorizes Matthew's longer version is doing something good; the reader who memorizes Luke's shorter version is doing something good; the reader who internalizes the rhythm of both and prays in their own words is closest to what the Teacher actually had in mind.

🎯 Street Level
The Help-Line Voice

There is a voice we use on customer-service calls that we do not use anywhere else. It is bright, slightly elevated, full of unnecessary please-and-thank-yous. "Yes, I would just love to know if you might possibly be able to..." The person on the other end does not know who we really are, and the voice is calibrated for that. Then we hang up. Whoever is in the room with us hears something else entirely. "That didn't work" or "I'm exhausted" or "we're going to have to figure something out." The customer-service voice was for a stranger. The room voice is for the people who know us.

That is exactly the difference Jesus drew. The hypocrites had been praying in the help-line voice on the corner of Main Street. The volume was correct. The vocabulary was decorated. The audience had no idea who they really were, and that was the whole appeal. Jesus told the disciples to drop the voice in a closet with the door shut. Pray to the Father in the room voice. He is the one who knows. "Our Father" is not a more religious phrase than "yes, please" on a help line; it is what a child says at the kitchen table. The closet door is what makes the room voice possible. The Father has been listening for it the whole time.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
The Familect at the Kitchen Table

Linguists and family-communication researchers have a name for the inside-the-house dialect every close household develops over time. They call it a "familect." The term has been working its way through academic communication studies for a decade, and a wave of attention since 2024 (in popular psychology articles, news features, and a flood of viral TikTok videos in which couples and families translate their own private vocabulary for outsiders) has put it in front of a much broader audience. The pattern is consistent. Long relationships generate shared idioms, nicknames, mispronunciations that became canon, and one-word references that stand in for whole stories. Earlier studies in the Journal of Social and Personal Relationships found that couples who reported higher marital satisfaction also recalled using more shared idioms with each other. The "culture of two" was correlated with thriving, not with childishness.

The strangest part of the data is the brevity. The closer the relationship, the shorter the messages get. A spouse looks at a partner across a busy room and one raised eyebrow communicates a paragraph. A parent says one nicknamed phrase and a child stops doing the thing the rules of the house forbid. By contrast, when we talk to strangers (a customer-service representative, a job interviewer, a guest we are trying to impress), our sentences get longer, our vocabulary gets more decorated, our inflections get more deliberate. The same speaker carries two registers in the same body. We use one with strangers and the other with the people who already know us.

That is the linguistic shape of the prayer Jesus taught. The hypocrites had been speaking the customer-service register on the corner of Main Street, with extra syllables and nice-to-meet-you cadences. The Lord's Prayer is a familect. Nine plain lines. "Our Father" is the address a child uses at the kitchen table, in a household where the listener already knows everything you might say. The closet door is what makes the familect possible. Long, decorated prayers are what the household never needed.

✝️ The Christ Connection

The most radical word in the prayer is the first one. "Father." The Son has been calling Him that for all eternity, and now, in a Galilean field, He hands the word to a circle of fishermen, tax collectors, and zealots and says: this is your address too. The cross is what makes the address actually true. Adoption is not a metaphor in the New Testament; it is a legal fact secured by Christ's blood. By the time we reach Romans 8 on Thursday, Paul will say the Spirit Himself cries "Abba, Father" inside us, because the Spirit is teaching us to pray the very prayer the Son has been praying since before the world existed. The Lord's Prayer is therefore not a polite way to start a religious exercise. It is the family prayer book of those who have been adopted into the household of God by the Son's life, death, and resurrection. We do not pray "Our Father" because we are very religious. We pray it because Jesus made it true.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Tomorrow morning, before you read another word, pray the Lord's Prayer slowly out loud in your private room with the door shut. Pause after each line and let your heart fill in the specifics. "Your name be honored as holy" with whom in your life today? "Your kingdom come" where, exactly? "Your will be done" instead of which of mine? "Give us today our daily bread" where in your budget or pantry? "Forgive us as we forgive" who, by name? Pray the prayer the way Jesus prayed it: short, sincere, and done before anyone else in the house is awake.
🙏 Closing Prayer

Father, we have prayed long prayers in front of people we wanted to impress, and silly short prayers when no one was watching, and sometimes nothing at all. Forgive us for the times we have used Your name as a stage prop and the times we have buried our heads in our pillows because we did not know what to say. Teach us today the way You taught the twelve. Show us the closet, the closed door, the early hour, the few honest words. Make "Our Father" the most personal sentence we know. Hallow Your name on our lips and in our days. Bring Your kingdom into the rooms we live in. Bend our wills to Yours, and trust us with daily bread instead of next month's. Make us forgive others the way You have forgiven us, even when it costs more than we want to spend. Lead us out of temptation and deliver us from the evil one. Because the kingdom and the power and the glory are Yours, today and always. We pour out our hearts before You, because You are our refuge. In Jesus' name, Amen.
