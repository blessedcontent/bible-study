📌 Week in Review

A week that opened with a psalmist who said the precepts had kept him alive ends with a Galilean wadi after a storm, two houses, and a question about whether the words we heard this week were also done.

  • ☩Sabbath: Sin, the Gospel, and the Law. We hung two Hebrew cabinets on the wall, the words for the wound (chattat, pesha, avon) and the words for the floor God put under our feet (piqqudim, mitzvot, huqqim, torah), and met a man under siege whose Memory Text said the precepts gave him his life back.
  • ☩Sunday: Distractions and Temptations. Samson "went down to Timnah" and never came back up. The Hebrew verb ra'ah opened his story the way it opened Eve's and David's. Sin does not start at the wedding feast. It starts at the first look that decides not to look away.
  • ☩Monday: Strongholds in My Relationship With God. Jesus the Surgeon used the Greek verb skandalizo, the trigger-stick on a hunter's trap, and prescribed a radical-cut response. Better to enter life maimed than to walk whole into the trash-fire valley south of Jerusalem.
  • ☩Tuesday: The Law. Paul named what the law does (it makes sin visible). John named what sin is (anomia, lawlessness). And Jesus, asked which command is greatest, folded the whole Old Testament onto two hooks: love God, love neighbor.
  • ☩Wednesday: The Law and the Gospel. Two Greek verbs in one sentence on a Galilean hillside, kataluo (demolish) and pleroo (fill to the brim). Jesus did not abolish the law. He filled it full of His obedience and absorbed its verdict in our place. Romans 3:28 is now ours.
  • ☩Thursday: Knowing and Doing. Two builders, two houses, one storm, and one Greek verb that separated them. Poieo: to do. The wise life is the one that digs to the bedrock before the rain. Hearing without doing is sand in a wadi.
🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
The Three Words and the Stone Beneath Them

The week assembled three Greek verbs and held them up against three Hebrew words, and the synthesis fits in one line. Pleroo, to fill to the brim, is what Jesus came to do to the law. Kataluo, to demolish, is what He did not come to do. Poieo, to do, is what the law-keeping life looks like on the inside of a person who has trusted Him. The Hebrew underneath all three of them is older. Torah, from the root yarah ("to shoot an arrow, to point"), is the directional gift God gave a people He had just rescued out of slavery, written into the architecture of their week, recited in their kitchens, sung by their psalmist. Chattat (missing the mark) is what the law diagnoses. Salah (the verb the Old Testament uses only of God forgiving) is what the cross supplies.

The Sermon on the Mount and the Decalogue at Sinai are not two laws. They are one law spoken by one Voice through two different microphones. On Sinai the Voice was in fire and smoke and the trembling of a mountain. On the southern slope of the Galilean hills the same Voice was sitting on a stone bench at the edge of a meadow. The audience in both places heard the same God say the same things, with the cross now visible between them as the bridge that makes living the second sermon possible for people who could not live the first one on their own. By the end of this week we have walked the full distance from Exodus 20 to Matthew 7, and the surprise has been how short the distance really is. The law was always pointing at the Person who would fill it. The Person has come.

📖 Deeper Questions

Five questions to carry the week into the next week. Take them slowly. The answers do not need to be performed; they need to be honest.

1
How does popular culture view sin? How should our church respond?
Matthew 5:17
☩ Open in Bible App
"Don't think that I came to abolish the Law or the Prophets. I did not come to abolish but to fulfill."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

The cultural air we breathe has been gradually rewriting "sin" for a generation. The word itself has been turned into a punch line on cereal boxes and dessert menus and bedroom-furniture commercials. The categories the Bible names plainly (lying, adultery, greed, contempt, idolatry) get re-narrated as "personality," "authenticity," "self-expression," "trauma response," anything except what Sabbath morning's Hebrew cabinet called them.

The church's response to this is not to match the culture's volume in reverse, scolding it from the sidewalk. The church's response is to live and to speak the news Jesus spoke on the hillside in the verse above. The Bible's standard has not been abolished. It has been fulfilled in a Person, and the same Person is offering His fulfillment to anyone who comes by faith. Cultural Christianity that softens the diagnosis ends up with no gospel to offer; cultural Christianity that hardens the diagnosis ends up with no Christ in the room. Both miss the verse. Jesus came neither to demolish the standard nor to leave us stranded under it.

2
When have you seen firsthand how sin destroys relationships with God and with others?
Matthew 5:18
☩ Open in Bible App
"For truly I tell you, until heaven and earth pass away, not the smallest letter or one stroke of a letter will pass away from the law until all things are accomplished."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

The verse Jesus speaks right after the abolish-or-fulfill line is the one most people skip past. Not the smallest letter. Not one stroke of a letter. The Greek refers to the iota (smallest letter in the alphabet) and the keraia (the tiny serif that distinguishes one Hebrew letter from another). Jesus is telling us that every commandment, every clause, every small mark of the law has weight and matters. Which is the reason the consequences of sin are not exaggerated when we feel them.

This question asks us to bring honest examples to the table. The marriage that ended over a sin one of the spouses kept treating as a minor preference. The friendship that broke when a betrayal was finally named. The parent who could not look at the child without flinching after the words that should never have been said. The church that fractured over a secret. None of those losses is the universe overreacting. They are the smallest letter and the stroke of a letter doing what Jesus said they would do. Sin is never just an interior matter. It always travels.

3
Has obedience to God's law been easy or hard in your life? What factors have contributed to this?
Matthew 5:17
☩ Open in Bible App
"Don't think that I came to abolish the Law or the Prophets. I did not come to abolish but to fulfill."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

The honest answer for most people is some of both, and the factors are usually less mysterious than we make them. Obedience is hard in seasons when our love for Jesus has gone quiet, when we are running on yesterday's fuel, when we are isolated from believers who know us, when we have stopped attending to the means God uses to feed faith (Word, prayer, the Sabbath, the table, confession, the body of Christ). Obedience tends to feel surprisingly possible in seasons when those means are running through us at normal pressure.

The verse above is the reframe that makes the question bearable. Jesus has already filled the law full of His own obedience on our behalf. The strength to obey is no longer the strength of our own willpower fighting a uphill. It is the strength of the One whose obedience has been credited to our account, now living inside us by the Spirit, expressing itself as the daily obedience the rescued life grows. When obedience feels especially hard, the diagnostic is rarely "I need more willpower." It is usually "I have drifted from the One whose fulfilling life is the source of mine." Come back to Him before the willpower conversation starts.

4
How can we, as people whose name shows how seriously we take the law, avoid the trap of legalism, or of depending on our law-keeping to save us? On the day of judgment, what are we going to rely on to stand in front of a holy and perfect God? Our law-keeping, or Jesus' perfect righteousness in our stead?
Matthew 5:18
☩ Open in Bible App
"For truly I tell you, until heaven and earth pass away, not the smallest letter or one stroke of a letter will pass away from the law until all things are accomplished."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

Run the thought experiment honestly. Stand in the courtroom. The Judge is holy. The bar is the law Jesus refused to relax even one letter of. What do you place on the table as your case for standing? Your Sabbath-keeping? Your dietary obedience? Your offerings? Your years of service? Any one of those is a good thing in its place, but none of them survives a single second under the bar of not one stroke of a letter. The law Jesus refused to lower will measure every line of our record, and our record will be found short.

What we place on the table, then, is not our record but His. Jesus's perfect obedience to every stroke of every letter, His "all things accomplished" completed at the cross, is the only case any of us has worth presenting. Our keeping flows out of His; it does not earn anything in front of the Judge. The way to avoid legalism is not to lower the bar; that just produces a softer false gospel. The way to avoid legalism is to look at the bar honestly, look at our own life honestly, and then look at the One who stood under the bar for us, and to rest the entire weight of our case on Him. The law-keeping we then do is the family resemblance of a rescued people, not the entrance fee of an applying one.

5
How can knowledge (or the lack of knowledge) impact someone's relationship with God?
Proverbs 24:3
☩ Open in Bible App
"A house is built by wisdom, and it is established by understanding."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

The Hebrew nouns here are chokmah (wisdom, skill in living) and tevunah (understanding, the ability to discern the shape of a thing). The picture is the architect and the foreman at the construction site, the two complementary kinds of knowing that together raise a structure that will last. A house with no chokmah is built on bad design; a house with no tevunah is built without the discernment to spot the cracks. Solomon, who wrote much of Proverbs and built the Jerusalem temple, knew the engineering vocabulary at first hand.

Knowledge in the Bible is never abstract data. It is the kind of seeing that builds the house of a life that will hold. Hosea named the absence of knowledge as the reason Israel was being destroyed (the Hosea 4 verses from yesterday's reading). Yesterday's John 17:3 said eternal life is knowing God and Jesus Christ whom He sent. The lack of knowledge that wounds the relationship with God is not the absence of facts; it is the absence of the relational, embodied, daily, lived-in kind of knowing that lets the Father shape the actual shape of our days.

Proverbs 24:13-14
☩ Open in Bible App
"Eat honey, my son, for it is good, and the honeycomb is sweet to your palate; realize that wisdom is the same for you. If you find it, you have a future, and your hope will not be dashed."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB

Solomon ends the same chapter with one of the loveliest images in the whole book. Wisdom on the palate is honey on the tongue. The Hebrew noun for "palate" is chek, the same word Psalm 119:103 uses to say the Word was sweeter than honey to the psalmist's mouth. The honey Solomon names here is not a metaphor for vague pleasant feelings about godly knowledge. It is the actual taste of a person whose interior life has been formed by reading God's words slowly, repeating them, carrying them, loving them.

The promise on the end is what makes the verse a gift. "If you find it, you have a future, and your hope will not be dashed." The relational knowing that comes through the Word is a future-securing knowledge. It does not just answer this morning's question; it builds the foundation that the storm of Thursday's Sermon-on-the-Mount parable could not collapse. The lack of knowledge that wrecks a relationship with God is the lack of this kind of sustained, sweet, daily palate-feeding. The presence of it builds a house that holds.

🌍 Still Speaking
Every Reformation Began at the Same Page

Trace the line through the Old Testament. Every time the people of God recovered their relationship with Him after a long drift, the recovery began the same way. The reformation under Hezekiah started when the priests reopened the temple and read the law again. The reformation under Josiah started when the scroll of the Law was found in the renovation rubble and read aloud to a king who tore his robes when he heard what his ancestors had been ignoring. The prophet Elijah called Israel back to the LORD on Mount Carmel with the question, "How long will you waver between two opinions?" The question was only meaningful if there was a settled standard against which "wavering" could be measured. Jeremiah, walking the streets of a city about to fall, kept appealing to the Scriptures of truth as Israel's richest inheritance. David's strongest years and Solomon's earliest years had one thing in common with all of those moments. They were years in which God's Holy Word was reverenced in the hearts of the people, and that reverence is what gave the nation the strength to fulfill its calling.

Every reformation in every generation since has followed the same pattern. The Word recovered. The Word read aloud. The Word taken seriously enough to do something about. The Reformation in sixteenth-century Europe started when Tyndale and Luther put the Bible in the hands of farmers and bakers in their own languages. The Great Awakenings in colonial America happened when preachers and people returned to the Book and let it judge them. Every revival in the modern world has been a generation of people opening Scripture again and discovering, sometimes to their alarm, what it actually says. The pattern is so consistent it may as well be a law of spiritual life. God's people drift. The Word is recovered. The people return.

Our generation is no different. The drift is the drift of every generation. The Word is still on our shelves. The question this week has been planting in our chests is whether we will pick it up not just to know its words but to do them, until the house of our lives is built on the rock the Builder Himself has supplied.

✝️ Christ at the Center of the Week

Walk the week's hallway one more time and notice the Face in every room. The Memory Text on Sabbath was sung by a man whose precepts had kept him alive, and the Person those precepts were always pointing at is the One who fulfilled them. Sunday's Samson is the strong man who could not save himself; Christ is the strong Man who emptied Himself and saved us. Monday's Jesus prescribed the surgical cut of the hand and the eye, and the next thing we saw was His own hand and His own eye absorbed in the surgery we deserved. Tuesday's law makes sin known; Christ stood under the law's verdict so that the news of Wednesday could be true. Wednesday's pleroo is one Greek word for everything Jesus did in His life and in His death. Thursday's wise builder dug to the rock; the Rock is Him, and the foundation has been laid for us before we ever picked up the shovel. Friday's reformation pattern has always pointed to a Word that became flesh, the Person at the center of every page. Ten pistin Iesou, the phrase we held in our pocket all of last week, sits next to "the commandments of God" in Revelation 14:12, and now we know how the two belong together. The law tells us what love looks like. The Cross tells us what it cost. The Spirit gives us the power to live the family resemblance.

✅ Weekend Practice
Some time this weekend, before Sabbath closes, take fifteen minutes with one Bible and one open page. Read Exodus 20:3-17 (the Ten) and Matthew 5:17 through 7:29 (the Sermon on the Mount). Take them in that order. Read aloud. Move slowly. Mark with a pencil the one commandment from Exodus 20 and the one paragraph from the Sermon on the Mount that the Holy Spirit underlines for you. Then write, in one sentence each, what you are going to do about each one next week. Not "what I am going to think about." Not "what I would do in a perfect world." What I am going to do, by Friday, in this actual life. Tape the sentence to the dashboard or the bathroom mirror. The Word read becomes the house built when it crosses from the page into the week.
🙏 Closing Prayer for the Week

Father, we close a week in which You walked us all the way from Sinai to the Sermon on the Mount and showed us, slowly, that they were one Voice speaking the same words. We have seen the Hebrew cabinet of words for our wound, and we have seen Your cabinet of words for the floor You put under our feet, and we have stopped pretending that one is more honest than the other. Thank You for not abolishing Your standard. Thank You for not leaving us under it. Thank You for the Son who filled it full of His own obedience and absorbed its verdict on our behalf, so that Romans 3:28 could read like our own names on a deed. Make us, by Your Spirit, the doing-people the rest of the world needs us to be. Pull our small reformations along the same line every reformation has ever followed: back to the Word, back to its weight, back to the Person it has always been pointing at. The law is not a burden. Your commands are not heavy. Make our hearts light enough to lift them. And keep us, when the storm comes, on the rock that is Your Son. In Jesus' name, Amen.
